# Medications and Public Medicines Corporation methodology

Evidence and calculation notes for the Medications chapter of the National
Health Assurance dashboard. Compiled 2026-07-19. Dollar figures are shown in
real 2024 dollars unless otherwise stated.

## What the chapter separates

The medication strategy has three distinct mechanisms:

1. National purchasing and formulary management negotiate prices across the
   complete covered formulary.
2. A public pharmacy claims utility replaces fragmented benefit processing and
   makes prices, inventory, fills, substitutions, and exceptions visible.
3. The Public Medicines Corporation, or PMC, manufactures a narrower portfolio
   of essential, shortage-prone, high-volume, or preparedness products.

The fiscal model's national drug-price reduction includes all three mechanisms.
The PMC estimate in the Medications chapter is an attribution of part of that
same total, not an additional saving.

## Current medication spending

### Retail prescription drugs

- **Value:** $467.0 billion in 2024, up 7.9% from 2023.
- **Scope:** Medicines dispensed through retail and mail-order pharmacies.
- **Source:** CMS, [National Health Expenditure Fact
  Sheet](https://www.cms.gov/data-research/statistics-trends-and-reports/national-health-expenditure-data/nhe-fact-sheet)
  and [2024 NHE
  Highlights](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/highlights.pdf).
- **Confidence:** High. This is the official CMS National Health Expenditure
  account.

### Retail plus clinician-administered drugs

- **Value:** Approximately $680–730 billion in 2024.
- **Scope:** The $467.0 billion retail line plus an estimated $212–264 billion
  of infused, injected, and other medicines recorded inside hospital and
  physician-service categories.
- **Source:** CMS NHE for retail spending; MedPAC, [Prescription Drugs section
  of the July 2024 Data
  Book](https://www.medpac.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/July2024_MedPAC_DataBook_Sec10_SEC.pdf),
  for the accounting distinction.
- **Confidence:** Medium. CMS does not publish one unified national total for
  all retail and clinician-administered medicines. The dashboard's fiscal model
  uses a $717.9 billion 2024-scale base, which lies inside this range.

### International price comparison

- **Value:** U.S. prices averaged 2.78 times prices in 33 comparison countries
  for all drugs and 4.22 times for brand-name drugs in 2022. U.S. generic
  prices were lower than comparison-country prices.
- **Source:** RAND, [International Prescription Drug Price Comparisons:
  Estimates Using 2022
  Data](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA788-3.html), published
  2024 and sponsored by HHS.
- **Confidence:** High for the measured price ratios. The ratios do not by
  themselves determine the saving available to a national purchaser.

## Why federal negotiation remains limited

The Medicare Part D noninterference provision, 42 U.S.C. 1395w-111(i), bars
the HHS Secretary from interfering in negotiations between drug manufacturers,
pharmacies, and Part D plan sponsors; requiring a particular formulary; or
instituting a reimbursement price structure, except through the newer
negotiation program. See the [current U.S. Code published by
GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/42/1395w-111).

The Inflation Reduction Act created a defined exception. It currently reaches
selected high-expenditure, single-source Medicare drugs without generic or
biosimilar competition. A small-molecule drug generally must have at least 7
years since FDA approval and a biologic at least 11 years since licensure before
selection. CMS negotiated 10 Part D drugs for prices effective in 2026, 15 Part
D drugs for 2027, and selected 15 Part B or Part D drugs for negotiations
leading to 2028 prices.

- **Sources:** CMS, [Selected Drugs and Negotiated
  Prices](https://www.cms.gov/initiatives/medicare-prescription-drug-affordability/overview/medicare-drug-price-negotiation-program/selected-drugs-negotiated-prices);
  CMS, [2027 Negotiated Prices fact
  sheet](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/fact-sheet-negotiated-prices-ipay-2027.pdf);
  CMS, [2028 Selected Drugs fact
  sheet](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/factsheet-medicare-negotiation-selected-drug-list-ipay-2028.pdf).
- **Confidence:** High. These are current law and program documents.

The 15 medicines with prices effective in 2027 accounted for $42.5 billion, or
about 15% of Part D gross drug costs, in 2024. CMS estimates that applying the
negotiated prices to that year's utilization would have reduced net spending by
$12 billion, or about 44%, before the Coverage Gap Discount Program adjustment.
This is direct evidence that negotiation can save money, but it is not a score
of a national all-payer purchasing system.

## Why a permanent public manufacturer is included

FDA identifies manufacturing quality problems as the most common cause of drug
shortages. Older sterile injectables are particularly vulnerable because few
manufacturers and production lines remain, capacity is slow to expand, and low
profitability can lead a company to discontinue a product. FDA can expedite
reviews and work with willing manufacturers, but it cannot require a company to
keep making or increase production of a drug.

- **Sources:** FDA, [Drug Shortages: Frequently Asked
  Questions](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-shortages/frequently-asked-questions-about-drug-shortages)
  and [Drug Shortages: Root Causes and Potential
  Solutions](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-shortages/report-drug-shortages-root-causes-and-potential-solutions).
- **Confidence:** High.

The operating precedent is Civica Rx, a nonprofit manufacturer created by
health systems and philanthropies. Its membership contracts use volume
commitments, equal member pricing, buffer inventory, and multiple contract
manufacturers. HHS studied the model in 2024 as a response to shortages and
high prices.

- **Source:** HHS ASPE, [Impacts of a Nonprofit Membership-Based
  Pharmaceutical Company on Volume of Drugs Sold and Drug
  Prices](https://aspe.hhs.gov/reports/impacts-nonprofit-membership-based-pharmaceutical-company).
- **Confidence:** High for the operating precedent; medium for transferability
  to a national public corporation.

Insulin provides a cost benchmark. A peer-reviewed production model estimated
profitable annual biosimilar insulin prices of $48–71 per person for human
insulin and $78–133 for analog insulin. Those estimates are product-specific
and are not applied to the complete portfolio.

- **Source:** Gotham, Barber, and Hill, [Production costs and potential prices
  for biosimilars of human insulin and insulin
  analogues](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6157569/), BMJ Global
  Health, 2018.
- **Confidence:** High for the modeled insulin products; low for extrapolation
  to other manufacturing platforms.

## The 200-family portfolio

The plan specifies 200 controlled product families. The list is intentionally
narrower than the complete national formulary. It includes exact essential
forms and assigns each family to a qualification phase based on manufacturing
complexity and operational priority:

| Qualification phase | Year anchor | Families qualifying | Cumulative total |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Phase 5 | Year 7 | 61 | 61 |
| Phase 6 | Year 8 | 116 | 177 |
| Phase 7 | Year 10 | 11 | 188 |
| Phase 8 | Year 12 | 12 | 200 |

- **Confidence:** High as a specified design obligation. These counts are not
  claims about current U.S. production.
- **Clinical qualification:** FDA and clinical experts still determine exact
  strengths, presentations, labeling, manufacturing controls, and
  interchangeability. Inclusion in the portfolio is not itself marketing
  approval.

The portfolio is selected using six reasons that may overlap: immediate
clinical criticality, high routine volume, shortage or concentrated-production
risk, price or access inequity, maternal and child need, and emergency
preparedness. The chapter exposes every family and its reasons through search
and filters.

## How public production would work

The PMC would act as a drug sponsor and manufacturer, not as an exemption from
FDA oversight.

1. Select the exact presentation and estimate national demand.
2. Secure an intellectual-property route through an expired patent, license,
   public-interest terms, or lawful federal use with compensation where
   authorized. Under 28 U.S.C. 1498, a patent owner's remedy for authorized
   federal use or manufacture is reasonable compensation. See the [GAO
   explanation of federal patent
   use](https://www.gao.gov/products/b-195193).
3. Qualify active-ingredient sources and a backup source.
4. Build or acquire a PMC line, or control an affiliate or contract
   manufacturer's line under the same public quality and transparency duties.
5. Submit the appropriate FDA application. Most conventional generics use an
   abbreviated new drug application, or ANDA. Biologics and some complex
   products use different pathways.
6. Pass FDA review and inspection, validate the process, release conforming
   lots, maintain inventory, monitor safety, and retain a separate qualified
   source.

- **Sources:** FDA, [Abbreviated New Drug
  Application](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/types-applications/abbreviated-new-drug-application-anda),
  [Generic Drug Approval
  Process](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/generic-drugs/what-approval-process-generic-drugs),
  and [Current Good Manufacturing Practice
  Regulations](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/pharmaceutical-quality-resources/current-good-manufacturing-practice-cgmp-regulations).
- **Confidence:** High for the FDA requirements; medium for the proposed
  organizational sequence.

## PMC savings calculation

The chapter reports an illustrative annual purchasing-savings attribution:

`2024-scale all-drug spending × PMC portfolio spend share × net acquisition-cost reduction`

The fixed all-drug base is $717.9 billion, matching the healthcare fiscal
model. Two user-adjustable assumptions define the attribution:

- **PMC portfolio spend share:** 5–25%, with a 15% planning midpoint.
- **Net acquisition-cost reduction on PMC products:** 25–55%, with a 40%
  midpoint. This is the same sourced range used by the fiscal model's national
  drug-price parameter.

The midpoint is about $43 billion per year:

`$717.9B × 15% × 40% = $43.1B`

The displayed stress range is approximately $9–99 billion per year. It pairs
the low values and high values, so it is a boundary rather than a probability
interval.

- **Confidence:** Low for PMC attribution. Claims data do not yet identify what
  share of national net drug spending the future 200-family portfolio would
  represent, and product-level production costs are not available for the full
  list.
- **Important boundary:** This is gross drug-purchase savings after the modeled
  acquisition-cost reduction. It does not score PMC start-up capital, saved
  shortage costs, adherence effects, or health outcomes. It is included inside,
  not added to, the fiscal model's broader national drug-price saving.

## Medicines outside public production

National purchasing remains responsible for medicines outside the 200-family
portfolio. The PMC would normally not manufacture:

- patented or exclusive single-source products without a lawful license or
  federal-use route;
- patient-specific cell and gene therapies whose production is inseparable
  from specialized clinical delivery;
- very low-volume products where a qualified supplier with an access guarantee
  is safer and less costly than a standing public line;
- products already served by a resilient, low-price, multi-supplier market
  when another line would add little bargaining or continuity value;
- noncovered cosmetic or non-medically-necessary products; and
- any product for which the PMC has not completed the appropriate FDA
  application, inspection, process validation, and lot-release controls.

These are operating categories, not a permanent exclusion list. A product can
move into public production if price, shortage, preparedness, or equity evidence
changes and the required legal and FDA pathways are completed.
