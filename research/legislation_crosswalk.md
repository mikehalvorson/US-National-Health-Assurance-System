# Legislation tab: legal crosswalk and drafting methodology

## Purpose and limits

The Legislation tab translates the legal architecture in the **National
Health Assurance Framework v2.0.0** into a public-facing implementation map.
It is a legislative planning tool, not legal advice, introduced bill text, a
fifty-state survey, or a completed constitutional opinion.

The framework itself draws the same boundary. Sections 9.2 and 9.3 specify
the method and major legal domains; Appendix L supplies a 19-title enabling
act architecture; OI-061 through OI-070 leave exact bill text,
jurisdiction-specific conforming amendments, constitutional validation, and
litigation analysis open. The tab therefore says “complete major-law-family
map,” not “every affected section has been validated.”

## Controlling framework method

Every legacy legal function must receive one disposition:

- **PRESERVE** — continue the existing authority or protection.
- **PARALLEL** — operate the predecessor and successor together during a
  certified transition.
- **TRANSFER** — move the function, record, obligation, funding stream, or
  pending case to the successor.
- **AMEND** — revise current statutory language.
- **PREEMPT** — displace only a state-law conflict within Congress’s valid
  authority.
- **SUNSET** — terminate a duplicative predecessor only after the required
  evidence passes.
- **FALLBACK** — retain or revive a safe statutory default if the successor
  cannot operate.

The transition sequence is successor-first:

1. Enact the new right, institution, funding source, remedy, and continuity
   rule.
2. Add section-by-section conforming amendments, savings clauses,
   severability, and revival fallbacks.
3. Fund and appoint the institutions.
4. Complete APA rules and voluntary, clearly conditioned compacts.
5. Run predecessor and successor systems in parallel.
6. Migrate people, records, money, providers, and pending cases in certified
   waves.
7. Sunset a predecessor only after the framework’s Gate 8 continuity evidence
   passes.

## Major-law-family crosswalk

The dashboard groups the framework’s federal conforming-change table and
state/tribal architecture into thirteen domains:

1. Coverage and public programs.
2. Civil rights, disability, conscience, and remedies.
3. Employer insurance and labor.
4. Tax, revenue, and trust funds.
5. Drugs, devices, diagnostics, and public production.
6. Hospitals, providers, payment, and program integrity.
7. Records, privacy, cyber, and AI.
8. Workforce, education, compensation, and scope.
9. Expanded benefits, emergency care, and public health.
10. State law, insurance, and household protection.
11. Tribal health, treaties, and self-determination.
12. Administration, appropriations, and continuity.
13. Competition, corporate structure, and research.

These domains include every federal law family named by the framework:
Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, ACA, FEHBA, TRICARE, VA; ERISA,
McCarran-Ferguson, insurance interfaces; the Internal Revenue Code and
payroll/reporting; the FDCA, patent law, Hatch-Waxman, Bayh-Dole, the Orphan
Drug Act, BPCIA, and the Controlled Substances Act; HIPAA, HITECH, the 21st
Century Cures Act, and 42 CFR Part 2; the Anti-Kickback Statute, Stark Law,
False Claims Act; the PHSA and Indian Health Care Improvement Act; the
Impoundment Control Act and APA; antitrust, corporate, nonprofit-hospital,
medical-debt, and consumer-credit law.

Additional statutes shown in the tab are necessary implementation interfaces
within those domains, including COBRA, EMTALA, the No Surprises Act, FISMA,
CIRCIA, federal employee and vacancy law, the Antideficiency Act, Prompt
Payment Act, Inspector General Act, FOIA, Federal Records Act, HCBS, PACE,
MHPAEA, Stafford Act, PREP Act, federal research law, and the Common Rule.

## Constitutional conclusion

### Amendment not required for implementation

The dashboard’s planning conclusion is that a constitutional amendment is not
required to create the proposed federal statutory system. Congress would need
to draft each operative provision around a valid enumerated power, principally:

- the Taxing and Spending Clauses for national financing, benefits, grants,
  and trust-fund architecture;
- the Commerce Clause for interstate healthcare, insurance, pharmaceutical,
  device, data, labor, and provider markets;
- the Indian Commerce Clause and federal Indian-affairs power for direct
  tribal implementation;
- the Necessary and Proper Clause for means rationally related to those
  enumerated powers; and
- the Supremacy Clause for valid, explicit, bounded preemption.

This is not a claim that any conceivable provision is constitutional. The
draft must still comply with anti-commandeering, Spending Clause clear-notice
and anti-coercion rules, Article II appointment and removal rules, due process,
takings, equal-protection principles, speech and religious-exercise law,
Article III, standing, and remedial limits.

### Current removal-law constraint

On June 29, 2026, the Supreme Court decided **Trump v. Slaughter**, No.
25-332. The Court held the FTC’s for-cause removal provision unconstitutional
and stated that officers within the President’s general administrative control
must be removable at will. The tab treats that decision as current law.

Accordingly, the framework’s executive hardening cannot rest on for-cause
tenure for officials who execute federal law. Drafting should instead rely on
the allocation of nonexecutive functions to constitutionally appropriate
legislative offices or courts, plus mandatory statutory duties, direct
spending, automatic defaults, protected evidence feeds, inspectors, standing,
expedited review, remedies, severability, and revival fallbacks. Any
non-Article III tribunal or nonexecutive scorekeeping design still needs a
separate Appointments Clause and separation-of-powers opinion.

### Optional amendment

The tab includes illustrative wording for an affirmative healthcare-right
amendment. It is labeled optional and not required. The wording deliberately
uses “as provided by law” so Congress retains responsibility for defining
benefits, financing, remedies, and implementation schedules, and it permits
states to provide greater protection.

Under Article V, Congress may propose an amendment by a two-thirds vote of
both houses, and three-fourths of the states must ratify. The President’s
signature is not part of the Article V proposal process.

## Federalism and transition drafting rules

- Do not require state officers to administer federal law.
- Make any state participation voluntary, clearly conditioned in advance,
  prospectively funded, auditable, curable, and noncoercive.
- Always provide a workable direct federal administration path.
- Use express preemption only for material interference with the national
  guarantee, payment rail, or required interoperability.
- Preserve state health and safety, licensure, discipline, public health,
  supplemental coverage, more-protective privacy and consumer rules, labor
  rights, and remedies unless a specific conflict requires otherwise.
- Conduct nation-to-nation tribal consultation before bill introduction.
- Use a separate tribal crosswalk, direct funding and compacts, and explicit
  nondiminution clauses; do not require a state intermediary.

## Legislative procedure

Regular legislation can carry the full rights, institutional, regulatory, and
remedial design. The revenue title should originate in the House. Congress
could use budget reconciliation for qualifying spending and revenue
provisions, but the Senate’s Byrd Rule permits points of order against
extraneous provisions, including provisions whose budget effects are merely
incidental to nonbudgetary policy. The nonbudgetary operating architecture
therefore needs a regular legislative vehicle even if budget provisions move
through reconciliation.

## Required completion artifacts

Before introduction, legislative counsel should publish:

1. A federal provision-object table with current citation, function,
   disposition, successor citation, effective date, transition condition,
   savings clause, and fallback.
2. A state and territorial annex covering insurance, Medicaid/CHIP, public
   employees, tax, licensure, scope, facilities, public health, labor, debt,
   privacy, and corporate law.
3. A tribal consultation and legal crosswalk covering treaties, trust,
   self-determination, service, funding, workforce, and data sovereignty.
4. A constitutional and litigation red-team memorandum.
5. CBO and JCT scores plus federal and state fiscal transition tables.
6. An operational effective-date schedule tied to the framework’s rollout
   phases and Gate 8.
