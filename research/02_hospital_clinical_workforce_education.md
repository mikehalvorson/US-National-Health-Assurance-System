# Research Baselines: Hospital, Clinical, Diagnostic-Treatment Network Unit, and Workforce Education Parameters

Compiled for calibration of the "National Health Assurance" fiscal simulation. All figures are sourced from named, dated, publicly available reports. Where no single authoritative number exists, a range is given with methodology notes. Confidence notes flag whether a figure is a hard official statistic, a survey-derived estimate, or an extrapolation/proxy.

## CP-HOSP: Hospital / Facility Parameters

### CP-HOSP-001 — Total U.S. hospital care spending
- **Value:** $1.5 trillion in 2023 (31% of total U.S. national health expenditures); grew 10.4% in 2023, the fastest rate since 1990 (+10.8%).
- **Unit:** USD, annual, national aggregate
- **Source:** CMS Office of the Actuary, National Health Expenditure (NHE) data, 2023 release (Dec 2024). CMS NHE Fact Sheet, https://www.cms.gov/data-research/statistics-trends-and-reports/national-health-expenditure-data/nhe-fact-sheet ; AHA News summary https://www.aha.org/news/headline/2024-12-19-cms-national-health-spending-increased-75-2023
- **Confidence:** High — official CMS actuarial statistic, the gold-standard source. Note growth driven by utilization/intensity, not price, per CMS. Total 2023 NHE overall was ~$4.9 trillion (7.5% growth); hospital share held near 31%.

### CP-HOSP-002 — Number of U.S. hospitals, by type
- **Value:** ~6,120 total U.S. hospitals; 5,129 "community hospitals" (AHA definition: nonfederal, short-term general and specialty). Of community hospitals: 2,987 nongovernment not-for-profit; remainder split among investor-owned (for-profit) and state/local government. 207 federal government hospitals; 654 nonfederal psychiatric hospitals; 120 "other" hospitals (mostly long-term-care/rehab).
- **Critical access hospitals (CAHs):** ~1,356–1,383 nationally (cross-source range; CMS certifies CAHs under a distinct designation, a subset of community/rural hospitals), located across 45 states (5 states — CT, DE, MD, NJ, RI — have none due to density).
- **Academic medical centers / major teaching hospitals:** ~226 "academic medical centers" (AMC) strictly defined; ~1,431–1,700 hospitals carry some "teaching hospital" designation; ~204 are both AMC+teaching; AAMC's Council of Teaching Hospitals and Health Systems (COTH) has ~375–400 member institutions (the "major" teaching hospital tier).
- **Unit:** count, facilities
- **Source:** AHA "Fast Facts on U.S. Hospitals" (2024/2025/2026 editions), https://www.aha.org/statistics/fast-facts-us-hospitals ; AHA Hospital Statistics dataset (ahadata.com); Definitive Healthcare HospitalView analysis (2025/2026) for CAH and AMC counts, https://www.definitivehc.com/resources/healthcare-insights/us-critical-access-hospitals and .../academic-medical-centers-us ; AAMC COTH membership pages.
- **Confidence:** High for AHA community-hospital totals (definitive annual survey). Medium for CAH/AMC counts — third-party aggregators vary by ±30 due to differing definitions; use AHA Fast Facts as the primary figure and treat Definitive Healthcare numbers as a cross-check.

### CP-HOSP-003 — Average hospital operating margin
- **Value (2024 full year):** median operating margin 4.9% (Kaufman Hall), up ~9% YoY vs. 2023; December 2024 monthly peak 7.6%.
- **Value (2023):** ~40% of hospitals posted a negative operating margin (improved from 53% in 2022); historic-crisis comparators: 42% negative in the 2001 dot-com downturn, 32% negative in 2008-09 recession.
- **Value (2025, partial year through July):** median operating margin 1.7% including health-system shared-service allocations, 5.3% excluding them — a 2025 low point. Full-year 2025 (through December) landed at 1.3% median, inclusive of shared-service allocations.
- **Unit:** % operating margin; % of hospitals with negative margin
- **Source:** Kaufman Hall National Hospital Flash Report (monthly series, 2024–2025), https://www.kaufmanhall.com/insights/research-report/national-hospital-flash-report-september-2025-data ; Becker's Hospital Review summary of Kaufman Hall data, https://www.beckershospitalreview.com/finance/negative-operating-margins-likely-for-39-of-hospitals-this-year-kaufman-hall.html ; sample is ~1,300 hospitals nationwide (not universe-complete, skews toward larger/system-affiliated hospitals).
- **Confidence:** High for the trend and general magnitude; Medium precision on exact percentages because Kaufman Hall's panel is not the full universe of ~6,120 hospitals and margin calculation methodology (with/without system allocations) varies month to month.

### CP-HOSP-004 — Rural hospitals at financial risk / closure
- **Value:** 417 rural hospitals identified as "vulnerable to closure" in the most recent Chartis analysis (2025/2026 cycle); >40% of all rural hospitals operating at a loss. Since 2010, 206 rural hospitals have closed or converted away from inpatient care (e.g., to Rural Emergency Hospital designation). Separately, Sheps Center counts 197 rural hospital closures/conversions since Jan 2005 (109 full closures, 88 converted), 154 since 2010. A third estimate (CHQPR/Sheps-adjacent) puts 338 rural hospitals "at particular risk" based on high Medicaid share or 3 consecutive years of negative total margin.
- **Service-line erosion:** 331 rural hospitals stopped offering obstetric (OB) services 2011–2024; 448 rural hospitals ended chemotherapy services 2014–2024 (10 states with >40% of rural hospitals dropping chemo).
- **Unit:** count of facilities; % at risk
- **Source:** The Chartis Group / Chartis Center for Rural Health, "Rural Health Safety Net Under Pressure" / "2025–2026 Rural Health State of the State," https://www.chartis.com/insights/2026-rural-health-state-state ; Cecil G. Sheps Center for Health Services Research (UNC), Rural Hospital Closures tracker, https://www.shepscenter.unc.edu/programs-projects/rural-health/rural-hospital-closures/ ; Center for Healthcare Quality and Payment Reform (CHQPR), "Rural Hospitals at Risk of Closing."
- **Confidence:** Medium-High. Different trackers use different closure/conversion definitions and windows, so treat 400–450 as the "at elevated risk" order of magnitude and 150–210 as the "actually closed/converted since 2010–2005" order of magnitude. Both Chartis and Sheps Center are considered the two most authoritative sources in this space and are frequently cited together in federal policy analysis.

### CP-HOSP-005 — Facility fee / outpatient billing markup vs. Medicare and vs. physician offices
- **Value:** Privately insured payers paid hospitals 254% of Medicare rates on average in 2022 (all hospital services, RAND Round 5.1). By service type: inpatient hospital facility services averaged 255% of Medicare; outpatient hospital facility services averaged 289% (some releases cite 279%) of Medicare; associated professional (physician) services billed alongside those facility claims averaged 188% of Medicare. Ambulatory surgical center (freestanding, non-hospital) prices for comparable procedures averaged only 170% of Medicare — i.e., materially cheaper than hospital outpatient department billing for the same service. State-level variation is wide: Arkansas lowest at 162% of Medicare, Florida highest at 346%.
- **Unit:** % of Medicare-equivalent price
- **Source:** RAND Corporation, "Prices Paid to Hospitals by Private Health Plans" (Hospital Price Transparency Study, Round 5.1), 2024, https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA1144-2-v2.html ; RAND press release May 2024, https://www.rand.org/news/press/2024/05/13.html . Underlying data: 2020–2022 private-insurance medical claims, employer-sponsored initiative funded by the Employers' Forum of Indiana/Arnold Ventures.
- **Confidence:** High — RAND's transparency series is the most widely cited independent source on this exact comparison, now in its 5th/5.1 iteration with large claims samples. Note: RAND does not report a clean "independent physician office" baseline separate from ASC/professional-fee figures; the professional-fee (188%) and ASC (170%) figures are the best available proxies for "non-hospital-owned" pricing versus hospital-owned outpatient departments (289%). This is the single best evidentiary basis for modeling a "facility fee markup" parameter distinguishing hospital-outpatient billing from independent/community settings.

### CP-HOSP-006 — Emergency department utilization: volume, cost, avoidability
- **Annual ED visits:** 155 million in 2022 (rate: 47 visits per 100 people). NHAMCS is the historical source but CDC discontinued NHAMCS after the 2022 data year; ED estimates continue via the National Hospital Care Survey (NHCS) going forward.
- **Average cost per ED visit:** ~$2,453 per visit (KFF/Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker analysis), of which ~$1,134 is the evaluation-and-management component (combined professional + facility fee). A separate UnitedHealthcare 2023 data point: ~$1,700 median allowed amount per visit for in-network claims. AHRQ HCUP historical data (2017, inflation-adjustable) is also available for methodological detail.
- **Low-acuity / avoidable share:** UnitedHealth Group brief: up to two-thirds (~67%) of the 27 million annual ED visits by privately insured individuals are "avoidable" (~18 million visits), potentially treatable in primary care; average ED cost for these conditions was $2,032 vs. $167 in a physician's office (12x differential); estimated $32 billion/year in aggregate system savings if diverted. Independent peer-reviewed literature is more conservative: one large study found 29.0% of ED visits avoidable/potentially avoidable (5.6% avoidable + 23.4% potentially avoidable); Canadian pediatric-ED literature shows low-acuity share rising from 33% (2008-09) to 41% (2011-12) of volume.
- **Unit:** count (visits/year); USD per visit; % of visits
- **Source:** CDC/NCHS, "Estimates of Emergency Department Visits in the United States, 2016–2022," https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/dhcs/ed-visits/index.htm ; Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker, "How do facility fees contribute to rising emergency department costs?" https://www.healthsystemtracker.org/brief/how-do-facility-fees-contribute-to-rising-emergency-department-costs/ ; UnitedHealth Group, "18 Million Avoidable Hospital Emergency Department Visits" brief (2019, still the most-cited figure as of 2024-2026 secondary coverage), https://www.unitedhealthgroup.com/content/dam/UHG/PDF/2019/UHG-Avoidable-ED-Visits.pdf ; peer-reviewed avoidability studies via PMC (Health Services Research, 2022).
- **Confidence:** High for visit volume (CDC official). Medium for average cost (multiple competing estimates, no single "official" number — recommend using $2,000–$2,500 as central range). Medium-Low for "avoidable share" — the widely-cited "two-thirds" UnitedHealth figure is an insurer-funded brief (methodologically an outlier on the high end) vs. independent peer-reviewed work clustering closer to 25-30%; recommend modeling a 25%-50% range with 30% as a defensible central estimate and flagging the 67% figure as an upper-bound/advocacy number.

### CP-HOSP-007 — Hospital administrative cost share (int'l comparison)
- **Value:** U.S. hospital administrative costs = 25.3% of total hospital expenditures in 2011 (up from 23.5%/$97.8B in 2000 to 25.3%/$215.4B in 2011), the highest of 8 nations studied. Comparators: Netherlands 19.8%, England 15.5%, Wales/Scotland and Canada markedly lower (Scotland and Canada — both single-payer global-budget systems — had the lowest administrative cost shares in the study).
- A related, broader (not hospital-specific) Himmelstein/Woolhandler/Campbell finding (Annals of Internal Medicine, Jan 2020): U.S. total health-system administrative spending was 34.2% of national health expenditures in 2017 vs. 17.0% in Canada — i.e., administrative costs are roughly double the Canadian share when measured system-wide (not just hospitals). An earlier landmark 1999-data comparison found U.S. administration at 31% of total health spending vs. 16.7% in Canada.
- **Unit:** % of hospital (or total health system) expenditure
- **Source:** Himmelstein DU, Jun M, Busse R, et al., "A Comparison of Hospital Administrative Costs in Eight Nations: US Costs Exceed All Others By Far," Health Affairs 33(9), 2014, DOI 10.1377/hlthaff.2013.1327, https://www.healthaffairs.org/doi/10.1377/hlthaff.2013.1327 ; Himmelstein DU, Campbell T, Woolhandler S, "Health Care Administrative Costs in the United States and Canada, 2017," Annals of Internal Medicine, Jan 2020 (PNHP summary: https://pnhp.org/news/u-s-far-out-in-front-again-in-hospital-administrative-waste/ ).
- **Confidence:** High — this is the canonical, most-cited academic source for this exact comparison in single-payer policy literature; explicitly matches the framework's methodology need. Note the hospital-specific figure (25.3%, based on 2011 data) is somewhat dated; the more recent 2017-data, system-wide figure (34.2%) is a reasonable current proxy if a hospital-only 2020s estimate is not available. Flag to the coding agent: no fully updated (2020s) hospital-only administrative-cost-share re-run of this exact methodology was found in this search; treat 25-34% as the plausible range depending on scope (hospital-only vs. system-wide) and note a Health Affairs Scholar 2024 piece flags ongoing gaps in the *availability* of consistent public hospital administrative-expense data, https://academic.oup.com/healthaffairsscholar/article/3/5/qxaf069/8140682 — itself a useful citation for "data availability caveat" documentation in your simulation's methodology notes.

## CP-CLIN: Physician / Clinical Services Parameters

### CP-CLIN-001 — Total U.S. physician/clinical services spending
- **Value:** $978 billion in 2023, +7.4% YoY (accelerating from +4.6% in 2022). Private insurance spending on physician/clinical services grew 9.4% in 2023; out-of-pocket spending on physician/clinical services grew 7%.
- **Unit:** USD, annual, national aggregate
- **Source:** CMS NHE 2023 data (same release as CP-HOSP-001), https://www.cms.gov/data-research/statistics-trends-and-reports/national-health-expenditure-data/nhe-fact-sheet
- **Confidence:** High — official CMS actuarial figure.

### CP-CLIN-002 — Physician compensation by specialty
- **Value (Medscape 2025-26 report, self-reported survey):** overall average physician compensation $386,000 (2025), +3% YoY. Primary care average $298,000; specialists average $417,000. Highest-paid specialties (2025-26): orthopedics/orthopedic surgery $611,000; cardiology $575,000; radiology $571,000; plastic surgery $554,000; anesthesiology $543,000; urology $535,000; gastroenterology $530,000; otolaryngology $508,000. Compensation declined YoY in several specialties: PM&R, nephrology, dermatology (-1%); oncology, hematology, pulmonology (-2%); psychiatry, allergy/immunology (-3%).
- **Value (MGMA 2024/2025 report, practice-management payroll data — generally considered more methodologically rigorous than self-report surveys):** primary care median total compensation ~$311,666 (2024 survey, 2023 data) rising to ~$329,780 (2025 survey, 2024 data), +5.8% YoY. Family medicine (no OB): ~$259,000 median at 1-2 years' experience, ~$322,000 at 3-7 years. Sample size: >220,000 physicians and APPs (2025 survey).
- **Unit:** USD, annual compensation, median/mean by specialty
- **Source:** Medscape Physician Compensation Report 2025 and 2026, https://www.medscape.com/sites/public/physician-comp/2025 and .../2026 ; Becker's Hospital Review summary, https://www.beckershospitalreview.com/compensation-issues/29-physician-specialties-ranked-by-annual-compensation-medscape/ ; MGMA 2024/2025 Provider Compensation and Productivity Data Report, https://www.mgma.com/2025-provider-compensation and https://www.mgma.com/getkaiasset/252744ee-c63b-4a96-9211-8a5d6b908b39/MGMA-2024-Provider-Compensation-Data-Report.pdf
- **Confidence:** High for order-of-magnitude and relative specialty ranking (both sources agree directionally). Medium for exact point figures — Medscape is a voluntary self-report physician survey (~n=7,000-9,000, prone to some selection bias); MGMA is derived from actual practice-management/payroll data across a larger, more clinically-verified sample and is generally treated as the more rigorous benchmark in health-economics literature. Recommend using MGMA as primary and Medscape specialty-relative-ranking as secondary/cross-check. BLS OES wage data (May 2024 release) is also available as a third independent cross-check but was not separately queried in this pass — flagged as a follow-up source if higher precision is needed.

### CP-CLIN-003 — Number of active physicians and NPs/PAs
- **Value:** 1,032,365 active physicians in the U.S. in 2024 (304 per 100,000 population); of these, 866,460 are "direct patient care" physicians (255 per 100,000). 38.7% female; 25.6% international medical graduates; 23.9% aged 65+.
- **NPs (incl. nurse anesthetists/midwives, BLS combined category):** ~382,700 jobs in 2024, projected +35% growth through 2034 (NPs alone +40.1%, the single fastest-growing healthcare occupation).
- **PAs:** ~162,700 jobs in 2024, projected +20% growth through 2034.
- **Unit:** count (headcount, active workforce)
- **Source:** AAMC, "U.S. Physician Workforce Data Dashboard," 2024/2025 release, https://www.aamc.org/data-reports/report/us-physician-workforce-data-dashboard ; BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook, "Nurse Anesthetists, Nurse Midwives, and Nurse Practitioners" and "Physician Assistants," 2024 data / 2024-2034 projections, https://www.bls.gov/ooh/healthcare/nurse-anesthetists-nurse-midwives-and-nurse-practitioners.htm and https://www.bls.gov/ooh/healthcare/physician-assistants.htm
- **Confidence:** High — both AAMC and BLS are the definitive federal/quasi-federal sources for these headcounts.

### CP-CLIN-004 — Physician administrative burden / prior-authorization cost
- **Value:** Prior authorization consumes an average 13 hours of physician-and-staff time per physician per week; physicians complete an average of 40 prior-authorization requests per week; 40% of physicians employ staff dedicated exclusively to prior-auth tasks; 94% say PA contributes to burnout; 93% say it delays care; 74% report denials have increased over 5 years; 80.7% of denials are overturned on appeal (suggesting substantial "wasted" initial-denial friction). Survey: Dec 2024, n=1,000 practicing physicians (40% primary care / 60% specialists).
- **Broader billing/insurance administrative cost (BIR):** U.S. physician practices spend ~$82,975 per physician per year on insurance-interaction administrative costs, vs. $22,205 per physician in Ontario, Canada (~3.7x) — Health Affairs, Morra et al. Breakdown: U.S. clerical staff spend 53.1 hours/physician/week on insurance-related admin (45.5 hrs on billing, 6.3 hrs on prior auth) vs. 15.9 hrs/week in Ontario; nurses/medical assistants spend 20.6 hrs/physician/week (vs. ~2 hrs in Ontario). An earlier (2006-data) Health Affairs study found interaction costs of ~3 hrs/week physician + 19 hrs/week clinical staff + 36 hrs/week administrative staff, totaling ~$68,000/physician/year in added cost vs. a single-payer comparator system.
- **Unit:** hours/week; USD/physician/year; % of denials overturned
- **Source:** American Medical Association, "2024 AMA Prior Authorization Physician Survey," https://www.ama-assn.org/press-center/ama-press-releases/ama-survey-indicates-prior-authorization-wreaks-havoc-patient-care and https://fixpriorauth.org/2024-ama-prior-authorization-physician-survey ; Morra D, Nicholson S, Levinson W, et al., "US Physician Practices Spend Nearly Four Times As Much Money Interacting With Payers As Practices In Ontario, Canada," Health Affairs, 2011 (Commonwealth Fund summary: https://www.commonwealthfund.org/press-release/2011/physician-practices-us-spend-nearly-83000-annually-physician-administrative ).
- **Confidence:** High for the AMA PA-burden survey (large, current, widely cited by CMS/regulators in the 2024 prior-auth reform rulemaking). Medium-High for the U.S.-vs.-Canada BIR cost comparison (methodologically well-regarded but now 10+ years old as a headline dollar figure — treat $83,000/physician/year as a 2011-dollars estimate; note it likely undercounts current EHR/prior-auth burden and would be meaningfully higher if re-run today, since PA volume has since grown per the AMA 2024 data).

## CP-UNIT: Four-Unit Diagnostic-Treatment Network Cost Benchmarks

*(No exact real-world analogue exists for the proposed "Type A/B" community diagnostic-treatment units; the following real-world comparables — retail clinics, urgent care, FQHCs, and e-consult/telehealth programs — form a defensible interpolation basis, roughly ordered from lowest-acuity/lowest-cost to higher-acuity/higher-cost.)*

### CP-UNIT-001 — Retail clinic (CVS MinuteClinic-type) visit cost and scale
- **Visit cost:** $59–$199 self-pay per visit depending on service (basic screening/rapid test ~$59; vaccination $69-$139; simple acute treatment, e.g., pink eye/sinus infection, $79-$99; annual physical or more complex eval $109-$199). Typical "basic visit" band: $59-$129.
- **Scale:** CVS MinuteClinic operates ~1,028 locations (2024), ~63% market share of the retail-clinic sector; Kroger's Little Clinic ~226 locations; Walgreens/Village Medical ~173 (down from higher after 2024 closures of ~160 locations); Walmart exited the retail-clinic business entirely in 2024 (closed all 51 in-store health centers). Overall retail-clinic sector is contracting/consolidating as of 2024.
- **Unit:** USD per visit; facility count
- **Source:** ThePricer.org and CVS.com MinuteClinic official price list (https://www.cvs.com/minuteclinic/services/price-lists) for pricing; CNBC, "Why Walmart, Walgreens, CVS retail health clinic experiment is struggling," May 2024, https://www.cnbc.com/2024/05/28/why-walmart-walgreens-cvs-health-clinic-experiment-is-struggling.html ; ScrapeHero retail clinic location analysis, https://www.scrapehero.com/retail-health-clinic-locations-in-us-location-analysis/
- **Confidence:** Medium-High on list pricing (directly sourced from CVS's own published price sheet). Low-Medium on true unit economics/margins — no public P&L breakdown was found for MinuteClinic specifically; treat the CVS price list as a revenue-per-visit proxy, not a validated cost structure. The sector's 2024 contraction (Walmart exit, Walgreens cuts) is itself a useful data point suggesting thin or negative margins at scale for the retail-clinic model — worth flagging as a cautionary comparable for the framework's "Type A" unit economics.

### CP-UNIT-002 — Urgent care center visit cost and operating structure
- **Visit cost:** $150-$200 per visit typical cost range (2024 market data); average net profit margin ~15%.
- **Cost structure:** Labor (provider labor especially) is the dominant cost driver, ~85% of operating costs, and is effectively fixed (a center cannot operate without a provider present) rather than variable with volume. Typical annual operating costs for a single center: $500,000-$1.5 million (i.e., ~$42K-$125K/month); a separate estimate cites $60K-$100K/month operating cost range. Medical-supply costs rose sharply as a share of expenses: 5.4% of total expenses in 2018 to 15.2% in 2023. Staffing scales in bands by daily volume (e.g., <35 patients/day: 1 front desk + 1 back office + 1 physician + 1 manager; >60/day: adds a second front-desk/back-office FTE, a "fast tracker," and a PA alongside the physician).
- **Scale:** ~11,150-15,000 urgent care centers in the U.S. depending on source/year (UCA's own 2024 count ~11,150-11,877; industry trade press citing ~14,000-15,000 including all walk-in-care formats); grown from ~9,000 in 2016.
- **Unit:** USD per visit; USD/month or /year fixed+operating cost per center; facility count
- **Source:** Urgent Care Association (UCA) Benchmarking Report — Finance (2023 edition; the primary/2024 edition is paywalled to members, ~$500), https://urgentcareassociation.org/wp-content/uploads/Finance-v9.pdf ; UCA "Center Counts," https://urgentcareassociation.org/center-counts/ ; UCA 2023 Industry White Paper, https://urgentcareassociation.org/wp-content/uploads/2023-Urgent-Care-Industry-White-Paper.pdf ; Journal of Urgent Care Medicine (JUCM), "Tightening the Belt: Rethinking Costs and Efficiency in Urgent Care," https://www.jucm.com/tightening-the-belt-rethinking-costs-and-efficiency-in-urgent-care/ ; secondary financial-modeling estimates (FinModelsLab) for illustrative cost breakdowns.
- **Confidence:** Medium. The authoritative primary source (UCA's own annual Benchmarking — Finance report) is subscription/member-gated, so the figures here are drawn from secondary citations of it plus industry trade coverage; treat $150-$200/visit and ~15% margin as reasonable central estimates but flag that a paid UCA membership or report purchase would materially improve precision if budget allows.

### CP-UNIT-003 — Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) cost-per-visit and funding — best real-world analogue for "Type A/B" units
- **Cost/reimbursement per visit:** FQHCs are reimbursed via a Prospective Payment System (PPS) using an all-inclusive national per-visit rate (adjusted for geography, urban/rural, and a +34.16% new-patient/wellness-visit adjustment), transitioned to PPS starting Oct 2014. State Medicaid FQHC rates illustrate the order of magnitude: e.g., Georgia's FQHC Medicaid rate for FY2025-26 is $141.22/visit. (Rates vary materially by state; national average all-payer cost-per-visit figures were not found as a single clean CMS/HRSA statistic in this pass — see caveat below.)
- **Revenue mix / funding gap:** In 2024, federal Section 330 grant funding per uninsured patient averaged $906 — below per-patient revenue from Medicare ($1,528), Medicaid ($1,418), and private insurance ($951) for the same population. Section 330 grants fall short of the true cost of caring for uninsured patients by roughly $500/patient, and base grant funding has not increased in a decade (as of the report). Section 330 funding = ~11% of total health-center revenue; other grants/contracts = ~15%.
- **Unit:** USD per visit; USD per patient (revenue by payer)
- **Source:** National Association of Community Health Centers (NACHC), "Community Health Center Chartbook" (2024 edition, analyzing 2022 UDS data) and "Health Center Funding Policy Paper" (Dec 2024), https://www.nachc.org/resource/community-health-center-chartbook/ and https://nachc.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/NACHCHealthCenterFundingPolicyPaper_Dec2024_Final.pdf ; underlying raw data: HRSA Uniform Data System (UDS), https://data.hrsa.gov/topics/health-centers/uds ; Georgia DCH FQHC rate schedule as a state-level cross-check, https://hfs.illinois.gov (Illinois) and https://dch.georgia.gov (Georgia).
- **Confidence:** Medium-High on the funding-gap and payer-mix figures (NACHC/HRSA UDS is the authoritative national data source). Medium-Low on a single clean national "average cost per visit" dollar figure — this search did not surface one clean HRSA/NACHC national average; the coding agent should pull the raw HRSA UDS national rollup table (data.hrsa.gov, "Cost" category in the 5-year national summary) directly for a precise figure, likely in the $200-$300/visit range based on state Medicaid PPS rates and general FQHC literature, but this should be verified against the primary UDS dataset rather than taken from this secondary research pass. **This is flagged as the single highest-value follow-up data pull**, since the prompt correctly identifies FQHCs as the best real-world analogue for the proposed unit network.

### CP-UNIT-004 — Telehealth / e-consult visit cost
- **Direct visit-cost comparison:** Telemedicine visits cost roughly 5x less than in-person visits in a large Penn Medicine claims study (Jan-Apr 2024, >163,000 visits): mean 30-day episode charge $96.60 (telemedicine) vs. $509.21 (in-person). Condition-specific: respiratory-symptom episodes ~$800 cheaper via telemedicine; mental-health episode costs were roughly equivalent between telemedicine and in-person. Telemedicine visits were associated with 23% fewer follow-up visits per 30-day episode (3.44 vs. 4.44).
- **Patient cost-sharing:** 2023 KFF data: average copay ~$25 for in-office primary care visit vs. ~$10 for a virtual visit.
- **E-consult (asynchronous specialist review) savings:** Health Affairs-published evaluation of a Medicaid e-consult program found average specialty-related episode-of-care costs ~$82-$84 per-patient-per-month lower for patients routed through e-consult vs. direct face-to-face specialist referral; one program's annualized Medicaid savings were $578,592. Champlain BASE eConsult (Canadian analogue, widely cited methodologically) provides an independent costing framework. Wasfy et al. (cardiology e-consult) found 11.6% of e-consults still resulted in an "unrecommended" in-person visit within 6 months, i.e., an ~88% avoidance rate for the specific pathway studied.
- **Unit:** USD per episode/visit/consult; USD per-patient-per-month savings
- **Source:** Penn Medicine study (2024), https://www.pennmedicine.org/news/study-finds-telemedicine-visits-cost-far-less-than-office-visits and Becker's summary https://www.beckershospitalreview.com/healthcare-information-technology/telehealth/telehealth-costs-5-times-less-than-office-visits-penn-medicine/ ; KFF 2023 copay data (cited via Secure Medical secondary summary — recommend verifying against kff.org directly); Health Affairs, "Reduced Cost Of Specialty Care Using Electronic Consultations For Medicaid Patients," 2018 (still the most-cited figure in 2024-2026 secondary literature), https://www.healthaffairs.org/doi/10.1377/hlthaff.2018.05124 ; AJMC summary, https://www.ajmc.com/view/cost-savings-associated-with-electronic-specialty-consultations
- **Confidence:** Medium-High for the telehealth-vs-in-person cost differential (large recent claims study, directly on point). Medium for e-consult PPPM savings (well-cited peer-reviewed source, though the underlying study is Medicaid-population-specific and from 2018 — direction and rough magnitude are reliable, exact dollar figure may need inflation adjustment for a 2020s-dollars simulation). Note: the prompt's suggested UCSF/SFGH eReferral cost study was not found with a specific dollar-per-consult figure in this search pass (only qualitative "improved timeliness/appropriateness" outcomes) — the Health Affairs Medicaid e-consult study and Champlain BASE are stronger quantitative substitutes and are recommended as the primary citations instead.

## CP-EDU: Workforce Education / Training Pipeline Parameters

### CP-EDU-001 — Cost per residency slot (Medicare GME payments)
- **Value:** Total Medicare GME spending ~$16.2 billion (FY2020) to ~$21.2 billion (FY2023), paid primarily to teaching hospitals, covering Direct GME (DGME: resident stipends, supervisory-physician salaries, program administrative costs) and Indirect Medical Education (IME: an add-on payment reflecting the higher patient-care costs of teaching hospitals, calculated via a resident-to-bed ratio formula). Federal support translates to roughly $100,000+ per resident per year in aggregate (DGME+IME combined, order-of-magnitude estimate); more granular hospital-level data (2015) showed per-resident payment rates (the "PRA," a DGME-specific measure) ranging $105,761-$182,233, with 47% of teaching hospitals above the $150,000 mark used as the comparator rate for the federal Teaching Health Center GME program. Note these figures reflect only the Medicare-funded share of true residency-training cost; total program cost per resident (including Medicaid, VA, and hospital self-funded contributions) is materially higher and was not separately quantified in this pass.
- **Unit:** USD per resident per year; USD total federal outlay
- **Source:** CMS, "Direct Graduate Medical Education (DGME)" and "Indirect Medical Education (IME)" program pages, https://www.cms.gov/medicare/payment/prospective-payment-systems/acute-inpatient-pps/direct-graduate-medical-education-dgme ; Congressional Research Service, "Medicare Graduate Medical Education Payments: An Overview" (IF10960) and "Medicare Graduate Medical Education, 2025" (IF13088), https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF10960 and IF13088; MedPAC, "Does It Cost More to Train Residents or to Replace Them?" (Sept 2013 contractor report) for the $105,761-$182,233 PRA range, https://www.medpac.gov/wp-content/uploads/import_data/scrape_files/docs/default-source/contractor-reports/sept13_residents_gme_contractor.pdf ; Fierce Healthcare summary of the $150,000/$1.28B overpayment analysis, https://www.fiercehealthcare.com/practices/study-suggests-medicare-overpaying-1-28b-annually-to-support-residency-programs
- **Confidence:** Medium. The aggregate national totals ($16.2B FY2020 / $21.2B FY2023) are solid CRS-sourced figures. The per-resident dollar figure is less standardized — "~$100K+/resident/year" is a reasonable planning estimate consistent with the user's own prompt, but the PRA range cited ($105K-$182K) is specifically the *direct* GME payment-rate benchmark from a 2013 contractor study using 2015 projected data, not a single current official "cost per slot" figure; the coding agent should treat $100K-$180K/resident/year (Medicare-funded portion only) as the defensible range and clearly document that true all-payer training cost per resident is higher.

### CP-EDU-002 — Number of current U.S. residency slots and specialty distribution
- **Value:** 2025 NRMP Main Residency Match: 43,237 total positions offered (all-time high, +4.2%/+1,734 vs. 2024), of which 40,041 were PGY-1 (first-year) positions (also an all-time high). 2024 Match: 41,503 positions offered, 38,941 filled; 38,494 PGY-1 positions offered. Internal medicine accounted for a large share of 2024-25 growth (+671 categorical/primary positions in the 2024 cycle alone).
- **Unit:** count, annual residency positions
- **Source:** National Resident Matching Program (NRMP), "Results and Data: 2025 Main Residency Match" and "...2024 Main Residency Match," https://www.nrmp.org/match-data/2025/05/results-and-data-2025-main-residency-match/ and https://www.nrmp.org/match-data/2024/06/results-and-data-2024-main-residency-match/ ; Advance Data Tables 2025, https://www.nrmp.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Advance_Data_Tables_2025.pdf
- **Confidence:** High — NRMP is the definitive, complete-census source for U.S. residency Match data (note: excludes some military and a handful of non-Match specialty positions, a small undercount of true ACGME total slots, which run somewhat higher when counting all PGY years across the ACGME's own "Data Resource Book").

### CP-EDU-003 — Medical school cost / debt
- **Value (2024-25 academic year):** average annual cost of attendance $59,720; 4-year total tuition+fees+insurance average $228,959. Public school 4-year total (class of 2026 cohort) $297,745; private school 4-year total $408,150. 2025-26 academic year average annual cost $61,110 (+2.33% YoY).
- **Debt (Class of 2025 graduates):** 70% of graduates carried debt; average educational debt among debtors $223,130 (+5% YoY). Median medical-school-specific debt $200,000; median pre-med debt $28,000. Public-school graduates: average total education debt $210,147 (+3% YoY).
- **Unit:** USD (cost of attendance; debt at graduation)
- **Source:** AAMC, "Physician Education Debt and the Cost to Attend Medical School," https://www.aamc.org/data-reports/students-residents/report/physician-education-debt-and-cost-attend-medical-school ; AAMC Debt/Costs/Loan Repayment Fact Card, https://store.aamc.org/downloadable/download/sample/sample_id/652/ ; AAMC MSAR Tuition, Fees and Insurance data, https://students-residents.aamc.org/system/files/2026-04/MSAR015%20-%20MSAR%20Tuition,Fees%20and%20Insurance%20Information.pdf
- **Confidence:** High — AAMC's Graduation Questionnaire and MSAR/tuition datasets are the authoritative, near-census-complete national sources for this exact metric.

### CP-EDU-004 — Physician/specialist shortage projections ("Specialist Bottleneck Index" inputs)
- **Overall:** AAMC projects a U.S. physician shortfall of 13,500 to 86,000 physicians by 2036 (wide range reflects "status quo" vs. "elevated need/improved access" utilization scenarios).
  - Primary care: shortage of 20,200 to 40,400.
  - Surgical specialties (general surgery, colorectal, neurosurgery, OB-GYN, ophthalmology, orthopedic surgery, ENT, plastic surgery, thoracic, urology, vascular, other): shortage of 10,100 to 19,900 (general-surgery projected surpluses partially offset larger shortages elsewhere in this bucket).
  - Medical specialties (broad non-surgical specialist bucket): ranges from a shortage of 5,500 to a *surplus* of 3,700, depending on scenario (e.g., critical care/pulmonology and endocrinology may see surpluses).
  - Structural drivers: U.S. population +8.4% by 2036, with the 65+ population +34.1% over the same period; >1/3 of currently active physicians are near retirement age.
- **Psychiatry (specific bottleneck specialty):** HRSA (Jan 2026 release) projects a national adult-psychiatrist shortage of 36,780 (status-quo scenario) to 86,430 (elevated-need scenario) by 2038; headline "43,660 by 2038" figure often cited as a blended/illustrative number. Psychiatry residency programs currently graduate ~1,500 new psychiatrists/year against an estimated annual need of 4,665-7,828 through 2037 under the unmet-need scenario. Child/adolescent psychiatry: potential unmet need of 7,470 by 2036 (2023 HRSA update). Behavioral-health demand overall projected +49% through 2033 vs. only +11% workforce-supply growth over the same period.
- **OB-GYN (specific bottleneck specialty):** workforce "adequacy" (supply/demand ratio) projected to fall from 93.4% (2025) to 81.7% (2037), i.e., demand rising while supply falls, per recent geographic-variation forecasting literature (PMC, 2025/2026).
- **Unit:** headcount shortfall (physicians); % adequacy
- **Source:** AAMC, "The Complexities of Physician Supply and Demand: Projections From 2021 to 2036" (June 2024 release, the current edition as of this research), https://www.aamc.org/media/75236/download and https://www.aamc.org/news/press-releases/new-aamc-report-shows-continuing-projected-physician-shortage ; HRSA Bureau of Health Workforce, "State of the Behavioral Health Workforce, 2025" and psychiatrist-shortage projections (Jan 2026), https://bhw.hrsa.gov/sites/default/files/bureau-health-workforce/data-research/Behavioral-Health-Workforce-Brief-2025.pdf ; OB-GYN workforce forecast, PMC, "Geographic variation in supply, demand, and adequacy of the obstetrics and gynecology physician workforce," https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12906581/
- **Confidence:** High for AAMC's overall/primary-care/surgical/medical-specialty ranges — this is the single most-cited, methodologically transparent projection series in U.S. health workforce policy and is explicitly the source the user's prompt names. High for HRSA's psychiatry-specific figures (federal projection model, most current available release). Medium for OB-GYN adequacy percentages (single recent study, not yet as widely cross-validated as the AAMC/HRSA flagship series, but directly on point and peer-reviewed).

### CP-EDU-005 — Nursing / advanced-practice workforce shortage
- **Value:** HRSA projects a national RN deficit of ~78,000 by 2025, narrowing slightly to ~63,000 by 2030; shortage severity projected to run ~10% (2027, the projected peak) declining to ~8% (2032) and ~6% (2037), with supply not meeting demand nationally until 2035. Sharp geographic disparity: non-metro areas projected at a 24% RN shortage in 2027 vs. 7% in metro areas. By 2035, 42 states are still projected to have some RN shortfall (some meeting only ~84% of needed workforce); West Coast and most Southern states (except MS and AL) not projected to reach 100% adequacy until 2036 or later.
- **Unit:** headcount shortfall; % shortage/adequacy
- **Source:** HRSA Bureau of Health Workforce, "Nurse Workforce Projections, 2021-2036" (National Center for Health Workforce Analysis, Dec 2025 fact sheet), https://bhw.hrsa.gov/sites/default/files/bureau-health-workforce/data-research/nursing-projections-factsheet.pdf ; AONL summary, https://www.aonl.org/news/HRSA-predicts-nursing-shortage-through-2037
- **Confidence:** High — HRSA's National Center for Health Workforce Analysis nurse-supply model is the standard federal reference for this metric, and this is its most recent (Dec 2025) release.

## Summary of Data-Quality Caveats for the Coding Agent

1. **CMS NHE and AAMC/BLS federal statistics (CP-HOSP-001/002, CP-CLIN-001/003, CP-EDU-002/003) are the highest-confidence figures** — treat these as near-authoritative and use point estimates directly.
2. **Kaufman Hall, Chartis, and Sheps Center figures (CP-HOSP-003/004) are high-quality but sample-based or definition-dependent** — model as ranges, not point estimates.
3. **RAND's facility-fee markup data (CP-HOSP-005) and the Himmelstein/Woolhandler administrative-cost studies (CP-HOSP-007) are the strongest available direct evidence for the single-payer-transition cost-savings mechanisms** the simulation likely wants to model (reduced facility-fee arbitrage, reduced administrative overhead) — prioritize these for the core savings logic.
4. **Retail clinic / urgent care unit economics (CP-UNIT-001/002) are the weakest-sourced category** — public data is thin because these are largely privately held business lines within CVS/Walgreens/UCA-member companies; treat as directional ranges only, and note that FQHC data (CP-UNIT-003) is likely the more defensible quantitative anchor per the user's own framing.
5. **The FQHC national average cost-per-visit figure (CP-UNIT-003) was not pinned to a single clean number in this pass** and is flagged as the top-priority follow-up: pull directly from HRSA's UDS national rollup at data.hrsa.gov.
6. **Where Medscape (survey-based) and MGMA (payroll-based) physician compensation figures diverge (CP-CLIN-002), prefer MGMA** for the simulation's core compensation parameters and use Medscape only for specialty-relative ranking/trend direction.
