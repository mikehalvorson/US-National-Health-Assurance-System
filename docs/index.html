National Health Assurance & Public Financing Simulator

Two working models built from public data: a fiscal simulation of the proposed National Health Assurance Framework, a public system replacing U.S. private health insurance with automatic coverage and zero point-of-care cost, and a general U.S. tax model showing how any funding need can be financed, and who bears the burden across income groups. Every parameter is sourced; every major assumption is adjustable. The models compute their own answers.

All results in real 2024 dollars. Calibrated to CMS National Health Expenditure data (2023, the last finalized year) and CBO's household income and federal tax distribution. Sources & methodology on GitHub.

The system today

American healthcare is not one system. It is four payment systems stapled together, with your coverage determined mostly by your job, your age, and your income. About 157 million people get insurance through an employer, who pays most of the premium out of what would otherwise be wages. Medicare covers ~67 million people over 65 or disabled, funded by payroll taxes, general federal revenue, and enrollee premiums. Medicaid & CHIP cover ~72 million lower-income people, funded jointly by Washington and the states. Everyone also pays directly at the point of care: deductibles, copays, and uncovered bills totaled $506B in 2023. And ~26 million people have no coverage at all. What's wrong, by the numbers

The problem is not that America spends heavily on health; every rich country does. It's that America pays far more and gets less coverage, less security, and far more overhead. Every figure below is sourced (citations in the research repository).

The fixable part

Most of that dysfunction is structural rather than medical, which means it can be fixed by redesigning how money moves rather than by rationing care. Four levers do most of the work:

  • Administrative consolidation. One public payer and one claims rail replace ~900 insurers' duplicated overhead and the billing bureaucracy providers maintain to fight them. Recoverable savings estimates in the literature run $200 to $500B a year.
  • Drug-price negotiation and public manufacturing. The IRA already proved government negotiation works (CBO-scored, 38–79% price cuts on the first ten drugs). Applied system-wide against prices 2.78× the international average, plus a public manufacturer for essential generics, modeled on Civica Rx's real $30-a-vial insulin. The model tests net price cuts of 25 to 55%.
  • Hospital payment reform. Global budgets replace per-claim billing, ending facility-fee markups, related-party extraction, and a slice of the $76–101B/yr in scored low-value care, while readiness funding protects ERs and rural service lines instead of letting them close.
  • Front-door capacity. A community diagnostic-treatment network and specialist e-consults absorb the routine care that currently lands in $2,453-average ER visits and months-long specialist queues.

The framework's bet: these levers can pay for most of covering everyone with no bills. Whether that holds is what the model on this page computes; it is not assumed.

The proposal: National Health Assurance

A single public system replacing employment-linked insurance entirely, specified in the framework document this dashboard simulates:

  • Automatic, continuous coverage for every resident: no enrollment cliffs, no churn, no linkage to your job.
  • $0 at the point of care for covered medically necessary care; medical debt for covered care is prohibited (patient billing capped at 0.5% of encounters).
  • National drug purchasing through a real-time public pharmacy claims utility (replacing PBMs), plus a Public Medicines Corporation manufacturing essential generics.
  • Hospitals on global budgets under public-service charters, funded for readiness and essential service lines, not per-claim revenue.
  • A four-type community diagnostic-treatment network (~15,000 units, from pharmacy micro-clinics to rural enhanced and urban public-health units) delivering same-day routine care.
  • A structured specialist backplane: complete workups and 24-hour e-consults before in-person referrals, with urgency classes and protected urgent capacity.
  • Expanded benefits phased in: long-term care (home-first), behavioral health, dental, vision, hearing, and EMS.
  • A 12-year gated rollout (2027–2038) with go/no-go phase gates, transition protections for patients, providers, and displaced workers, and independent oversight bodies.

Beyond dollars: what the model does not price

Some effects of universal coverage are well documented but deliberately kept out of the cost arithmetic, because turning lives and solvency into dollars invites false precision. They belong in the picture anyway. Each figure notes the NHA mechanism that addresses it.

The utilization-increase parameter in the model is the cost side of these same effects: people who stop skipping care start using it. The model prices that; it does not claim credit for the health improvements that follow.

Methodology and limits

What this is: a national-aggregate annual model. It computes the status-quo world and the NHA world category by category from CMS 2023 calibration data, phases policy in over the framework's Phase 0–8 roadmap, and samples every uncertain parameter from a sourced low/central/high range (triangular distribution). Draws share a systemic optimism/pessimism factor (weight 0.35): runs where benefit expansions overrun also tend to be runs where savings levers underdeliver, which widens the tails compared with independent sampling. Employer premium savings pass through to wages (adjustable, 40–95%, CBO convention) and the resulting income/payroll tax revenue feeds back into financing. Steady state is the 2040–2042 average.
On the framework document's original figure: earlier drafts asserted a mature cost of $4.75T/yr (2024 dollars) without a derivation. That figure is superseded by this model's computed projections; under central assumptions the model lands somewhat above it, and reaches it only under the optimistic scenario. The full comparison lives in the parameter table and scenario presets, not in the headline.
What it still is not: specialty-level queues and wait-time dynamics are not modeled, nor is provider behavioral response beyond the payment factor. Macroeconomic feedback stops at first-order wage pass-through with a fixed 28% marginal-rate revenue feedback; there are no general-equilibrium or labor-supply effects. The correlation structure is a single systemic factor, not a full covariance matrix. Geographic detail exists for the unit network (see the Unit Network tab) but the fiscal model's costs and prices remain national averages. Health outcomes are displayed with sources in the section above, not converted into dollars. Demographic aging is decomposed and shown under the cost-path chart, but the model does not simulate cohorts individually.
Weakest assumptions. These parameters have no direct real-world source and materially move the result. They are flagged low in the controls and listed with their derivation anchors in the full parameter table below.

Tax model methodology

Structure: income groups follow CBO's distribution format (quintiles, 80–90th, 90–99th, top 1%). Each instrument carries a sourced revenue estimate at 2024 scale (growing with the economy), a phase-in schedule, and an incidence vector (who ultimately bears it), using CBO/JCT conventions (employer payroll → workers' wages; corporate → 25% labor / 75% capital; consumption taxes → consumption shares).
Sources: the income-group table is CBO's Distribution of Household Income, 2022 (published January 2026), extracted from CBO's own supplemental workbook and inflated to 2024 dollars; revenue scores come from CBO's Options for Reducing the Deficit: 2025–2034 (December 2024, JCT staff), the Treasury FY2025 Greenbook, and the Saez–Zucman wealth-tax letter; health spending by quintile is BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2024 (healthcare runs 20.7% of income in the lowest quintile vs 3.7% in the highest; that is the regressivity this system unwinds). Full citations in research file 06.
Limits: incidence is static (no behavioral responses beyond the haircuts already in each estimate); group boundaries don't capture within-group variation (a "middle quintile" family with high health costs gains far more than one without); the wealth tax has no official JCT/CBO score and its legal survival is genuinely uncertain (the framework carries a fallback matrix for that reason); the estate instrument has no current score against the post-2025 $15M exemption; and CBO's December 2024 option scores predate the July 2025 reconciliation act, so some would re-base lower today.
