The system today

American healthcare is not one system. It is four payment systems stapled together, with your coverage determined mostly by your job, your age, and your income. About 157 million people get insurance through an employer, who pays most of the premium out of what would otherwise be wages. Medicare covers ~67 million people over 65 or disabled, funded by payroll taxes, general federal revenue, and enrollee premiums. Medicaid & CHIP cover ~72 million lower-income people, funded jointly by Washington and the states. Everyone also pays directly at the point of care: deductibles, copays, and uncovered bills totaled $506B in 2023. And ~26 million people have no coverage at all. Here is where the $4.87T came from and where it went. Hover any ribbon for the details.

What's wrong, by the numbers

The problem is not that America spends heavily on health; every rich country does. It's that America pays far more and gets less coverage, less security, and far more overhead. Every figure below is sourced (citations in the research repository).

The fixable part

Most of that dysfunction is structural rather than medical, which means it can be fixed by redesigning how money moves rather than by rationing care. Four levers do most of the work:

  • Administrative consolidation. One public payer and one claims rail replace ~900 insurers' duplicated overhead and the billing bureaucracy providers maintain to fight them. Recoverable savings estimates in the literature run $200 to $500B a year.
  • Drug-price negotiation and public manufacturing. The IRA already proved government negotiation works (CBO-scored, 38–79% price cuts on the first ten drugs). Applied system-wide against prices 2.78× the international average, plus a public manufacturer for essential generics, modeled on Civica Rx's real $30-a-vial insulin. The model tests net price cuts of 25 to 55%.
  • Hospital payment reform. Global budgets replace per-claim billing, ending facility-fee markups, related-party extraction, and a slice of the $76–101B/yr in scored low-value care, while readiness funding protects ERs and rural service lines instead of letting them close.
  • Front-door capacity. A community diagnostic-treatment network and specialist e-consults absorb the routine care that currently lands in $2,453-average ER visits and months-long specialist queues.

The framework's bet: these levers can pay for most of covering everyone with no bills. Whether that holds is what the model on this page computes; it is not assumed.

The proposal: National Health Assurance

A single public system replacing employment-linked insurance entirely, specified in the framework document this dashboard simulates:

  • Automatic, continuous coverage for every resident: no enrollment cliffs, no churn, no linkage to your job.
  • $0 at the point of care for covered medically necessary care; medical debt for covered care is prohibited (patient billing capped at 0.5% of encounters).
  • National drug purchasing through a real-time public pharmacy claims utility (replacing PBMs), plus a Public Medicines Corporation manufacturing essential generics.
  • Hospitals on global budgets under public-service charters, funded for readiness and essential service lines, not per-claim revenue.
  • A four-type community diagnostic-treatment network (~15,000 units, from pharmacy micro-clinics to rural enhanced and urban public-health units) delivering same-day routine care.
  • A structured specialist backplane: complete workups and 24-hour e-consults before in-person referrals, with urgency classes and protected urgent capacity.
  • Expanded benefits phased in: long-term care (home-first), behavioral health, dental, vision, hearing, and EMS.
  • A 12-year gated rollout (2027–2038) with go/no-go phase gates, transition protections for patients, providers, and displaced workers, and independent oversight bodies.

Federalism by design

Federal, state, and tribal authority must be separated precisely

The federal statute should operate directly where national uniformity is indispensable, preserve traditional state health and safety functions, and recognize tribal sovereignty and federal trust duties. It cannot force state officers to administer a federal program.

Federal direct authority

One national floor and a direct operating fallback

  • Coverage, benefits, payment rights, financing, national data and claims standards
  • Federal institutions, appropriations, remedies, audit, and continuity duties
  • Narrow express preemption when a state rule materially blocks the federal guarantee
State authority preserved

Health, safety, licensure, and more-protective rules

  • Professional licensure, discipline, facility safety, public health, and corporate law
  • Supplemental benefits and protections that exceed the federal floor
  • Voluntary administration through funded compacts with clear conditions and exit rules
Tribal sovereignty and trust

Direct nation-to-nation implementation

  • No diminution of treaty, trust, IHS, or self-determination rights

  • Tribal control of service design, data governance, workforce, and local integration

From bill text to live authority

How implementation would be performed

The legal transition follows the same safety logic as the clinical rollout: create the successor authority, prove it can operate, migrate by certified wave, and retire the predecessor only after continuity evidence passes.

01Close the legal crosswalk

Legislative counsel maps every affected federal section and each state, territorial, and tribal interface to a named disposition.

02Enact the 19-title act

Rights, institutions, mandatory funding, revenue, remedies, formulas, defaults, and transition authority take effect on sequenced dates.

03Amend legacy code

Congress inserts conforming text, savings clauses, severability, transition rules, and revival fallbacks before any repeal can operate.

04Fund and appoint

Trust funds, direct spending, appropriations, offices, inspectors, scorekeepers, courts, succession, and staffing floors become operational.

05Rule and compact

Agencies use APA notice and comment; governments execute clear, funded, voluntary compacts with data, audit, cure, and exit terms.

06Run systems in parallel

Claims, records, pharmacy, hospitals, and eligibility migrate in certified waves while predecessor benefits and pending cases remain protected.

07Sunset only after Gate 8

Legacy authority closes only after record, financial, workforce, appeal, continuity, and fallback evidence is independently certified.

Constitutional architecture

What the Constitution permits, constrains, and could optionally guarantee

Article I

Enumerated federal powers

Anchor financing in the taxing and spending powers; regulate interstate healthcare markets; use necessary-and-proper means tied to those powers.

Tenth Amendment

No commandeering or coercion

Administer federal duties federally. State participation must use clear, prospective, noncoercive funding terms and a workable direct federal option.

Article II

Executive power remains presidential

After Trump v. Slaughter, officers within the President's general administrative control must be removable at will.

Fifth Amendment

Process, property, and equal treatment

Give notice, hearings, review, compensation where property is taken, and rational, nondiscriminatory transition rules for patients and institutions.

First Amendment and RFRA

Belief does not erase patient access

Draft conscience rules narrowly, protect individual rights, and ensure an effective alternative provider and emergency-care pathway.

Supremacy Clause

Express, bounded preemption

State law yields only where Congress validly says so or a true conflict exists; explicit savings clauses prevent accidental field preemption.

Methodology and limits

What this is: a national-aggregate annual model. It computes the status-quo world and the NHA world category by category from CMS 2023 calibration data, phases policy in over the framework's Phase 0–8 roadmap, and samples every uncertain parameter from a sourced low/central/high range (triangular distribution). Draws share a systemic optimism/pessimism factor (weight 0.35): runs where benefit expansions overrun also tend to be runs where savings levers underdeliver, which widens the tails compared with independent sampling. Employer premium savings pass through to wages (adjustable, 40–95%, CBO convention) and the resulting income/payroll tax revenue feeds back into financing. Steady state is the 2040–2042 average.
On the framework document's original figure: earlier drafts asserted a mature cost of $4.75T/yr (2024 dollars) without a derivation. The full comparison lives in the parameter table and scenario presets, not in the headline.

Start with where the money is

Any honest financing conversation starts here. American wealth has concentrated to a degree the words "top 1%" no longer describe; the action is inside the top tenth and hundredth of a percent. That is why the model on this page breaks the top 1% into five bands, grows their capital faster than the economy (because it does grow faster), and includes instruments aimed specifically at them.

Why the ultra-wealthy are hard to tax

  • Income is optional at the top. The tax code mostly taxes realized income, and a billionaire can simply not realize any: hold appreciating assets, borrow against them for spending, and let the step-up at death erase the gain entirely. "Buy, borrow, die" is why measured top-0.01% tax rates fall below the merely rich.
  • Valuation and liquidity. Private businesses, art, and carried interests are hard to appraise annually, and taxing them can force sales; every wealth-tax design fights this.
  • A planning industry. Grantor trusts, dynasty trusts, valuation discounts, and pass-through games exist to move wealth around any single-instrument tax; the estate tax raises a fifth of what a static calculation says it should.
  • Legal uncertainty. A federal wealth tax faces the Constitution's direct-tax apportionment clause, and taxing unrealized gains tests the 16th Amendment's meaning of income (Moore v. United States left the question open, not answered).
  • Enforcement asymmetry. Audit rates on the largest returns collapsed for a decade; the IRS was outgunned by the taxpayers with the most complex returns and the most to gain from opacity.

How it can realistically be done

  • Tax what cannot flee or hide. Withholding and third-party reporting on capital income, an uncapped payroll base, and transaction taxes collect at the source, before planning starts.
  • Close the death loophole first. Ending stepped-up basis and taxing gains at death removes the exit that makes deferral a permanent escape; every rate increase works better once it is shut.
  • Minimum taxes on comprehensive income. The billionaire minimum tax sidesteps game-by-game whack-a-mole by asking one question: did you pay at least 25% on your true economic gain?
  • Tax inheritances as income to heirs. Constitutionally clean (it is income), progressive by construction, and aimed at dynasties rather than founders.
  • Fund the referees. High-end enforcement collects tax already owed; the return on IRS funding at the top is documented and large.
  • Coordinate internationally and at the exit. Pillar-2 minimum taxes, information exchange, and a real exit tax shrink the run-away option.
  • Build a legal ladder, not a single rung. The framework's own contingency matrix: if the wealth tax falls in court, fall back to mark-to-market; then to realization-at-death plus minimum taxes. Every scenario below survives losing its most contested instrument.

It has been done before

America taxed its top earners far harder than it does now, for decades, through its strongest growth years: the top marginal rate stayed at 91 to 92 percent through the entire Eisenhower administration and did not fall below 70 percent until 1982. It is 37 percent today.

The governance architecture

Replacing an industry means replacing its functions, and every function needs an owner. The framework specifies 82 entities organized under 9 governing bodies. The design follows two principles throughout: separate operations from oversight (the department that pays claims never judges its own appeals), and make the system hard to sabotage but easy to repair (mandatory payment rails, fixed-term appointments, vacancy-proof duties, an independent scorekeeper with a public formula registry). Each diagram below is one governing body; click any box for the full entry, including the position in charge and what it answers for.

Leadership titles follow standard federal practice: Senate-confirmed heads for benefit-paying executive bodies, with nonexecutive scorekeeping and adjudication assigned only to constitutionally appropriate legislative offices or courts. After the Supreme Court's June 29, 2026 decision in Trump v. Slaughter, this dashboard does not assume that an executive regulator can be insulated from presidential removal. "Replaces" identifies the function absorbed, not a claim that the named organization vanishes on day one; the Transition body manages every handoff.

The threat: dismantlement without repeal

A hostile executive would not need to win an immediate vote to repeal National Health Assurance. It could try to make the entitlement fail in practice: hold back money, leave leadership posts empty, hollow out staffing, delay or withdraw rules, suppress performance evidence, decline enforcement, or let contractors and state partners obstruct essential systems. The first harms would appear as interrupted medication, unpaid providers, closed services, broken appeals, and missing public reports.

Design boundary

Make unilateral executive hollowing visible, temporary, and remediable. The architecture cannot prevent Congress from changing the statute or a court from invalidating an authority. It can prevent ordinary executive inaction from quietly operating as repeal and can preserve lawful continuity while Congress and courts respond.

01

Starve lawful payments

Withhold or defer funds, delay apportionment, transfer balances, or pressure the payment rail to reprice certified obligations.

First visible harm: pharmacies, hospitals, and clinicians go unpaid.
02

Empty the offices

Decline appointments, manufacture quorum failure, impose a hiring freeze, or use a reduction in force below operating floors.

First visible harm: coverage, appeals, safety, and oversight clocks stop.
03

Sabotage by rule delay

Miss mandatory rule deadlines, withdraw implementing rules, or use new interpretations to narrow benefits and payment duties.

First visible harm: the same right produces different answers or no answer.
04

Stop enforcing the law

Ignore rights violations, staffing breaches, extraction, fraud, payment obstruction, or required corrective actions.

First visible harm: prohibited conduct becomes the operating norm.
05

Hide the evidence

Suppress required reports, block audit feeds, erase delay flags, or prevent independent bodies from seeing operational records.

First visible harm: deterioration cannot be proved or challenged quickly.
06

Break systems and partnerships

Terminate continuity contracts, let vendors retain data or code, obstruct state compacts, or disable the systems that move records and money.

First visible harm: local outages cascade into national service interruption.

Defense in depth

No single official, office, rule, contract, or court route is expected to carry the whole defense. Select a layer to see the attack it blocks, the continuity action that occurs without permission from the obstructing official, and the independent body that checks the result.

From interference to continuity

The framework does not wait for a discretionary self-report from the same chain of command accused of obstruction. It specifies a detection-to-remedy workflow driven by clocks, logs, obligations, staffing records, public-data completeness, escrow status, and complaints.

01

Detect

Automatic clocks and protected records identify a missed payment, vacancy, missing rule, suppressed report, or vendor refusal.

02

Classify

Separate lawful adjustment and ordinary incident from unlawful delay, nonfeasance, obstruction, suppression, or legal constraint.

03

Continue

Use the narrowest lawful default immediately: payment, succession, last-lawful rule, escrow release, offline mode, or federal step-in.

04

Compel

Start anti-impoundment or administrative action within 24 hours and notify the congressional, scorekeeping, and rights bodies.

05

Reconcile

Correct provisional actions without shifting error costs to patients, then publish the event, duration, remedy, and result.

Limits and unfinished statutory work

Open legal design

Leadership rules still require legislation

Framework open issue OI-019 leaves governing-body composition, appointment, terms, removal, quorum, conflicts, vacancy, and succession for final statutory design. Trump v. Slaughter now forecloses ordinary for-cause insulation for officers within the President's general administrative control; every body still needs an Appointments Clause and separation-of-powers opinion.

Operational proof

A fallback on paper is not enough

Each fallback still needs authority, an owner, funds, staff, systems, data, contracts, an activation clock, exercises, and a return-and-reconciliation path. Legal contingency instruments and recovery-time objectives remain subjects for specialized analysis.

Constitutional boundary

Hardening is not permanent entrenchment

A later Congress may amend or repeal the statute, and courts may constrain particular authorities. The design instead makes those changes formal and reviewable while severability and scoped fallbacks preserve unrelated coverage, payment, rights, and continuity functions.

Why the current health-data system fails

The United States has many capable electronic record systems, but not a dependable system-level record. Data exchange varies by vendor, payer, facility, and setting. A person can therefore have several incomplete versions of the same history while clinicians still lack the medication, result, discharge, or referral information needed for the next decision.

Current system problem leads to Framework response

The framework treats this as a safety and continuity problem, not merely an IT inconvenience. Its comparator records incomplete interoperability as a cause of duplicate testing, missing medication and history, failed follow-up, and migration risk. It also treats concentrated cyber and payment-rail failures as a separate national continuity hazard.

One national system, many authoritative nodes

The proposed National Health Assurance Information Mesh is a single governed data system, but deliberately not one giant database. Hospitals, laboratories, pharmacies, clinicians, regional systems, and public agencies may retain the records they are responsible for. The mesh supplies the common identity, locator, vocabulary, API, access, audit, correction, and cybersecurity rules that make those records work as one longitudinal system.

People are the capacity system

The framework cannot be staffed by moving boxes on an organization chart

Coverage, facilities, medicines, records, and specialist routing work only when qualified people are available in the right role, region, modality, and schedule. The framework therefore treats workforce as a hard constraint: headcount is discounted for leave, competency, schedule availability, retention, burnout, and unsafe overtime before it counts as effective capacity.

01

National shortages are geographically unequal

Nonmetropolitan nursing gaps, specialist scarcity, and long travel distances can leave a nominally covered rural resident without usable care.

02

Rural readiness is larger than visit volume

A low-volume community still needs observation, emergency handoff, pharmacy, transport, maternal and pediatric triage, and weather resilience.

03

Training arrives years after funding

A funded slot becomes capacity only after faculty, clinical rotations, completion, credentialing, and a matched service location.

04

Burnout destroys nominal capacity

Vacancies, turnover, leave, moral injury, and excessive overtime reduce safe capacity even when an authorized position exists on paper.

05

New benefits release hidden demand

Long-term care, behavioral health, dental, vision, hearing, EMS, and public-health access cannot expand faster than trained teams and safe facilities.

06

Administrative simplification displaces workers

Private claims, prior authorization, PBM rebate, brokerage, and revenue-cycle work shrinks before every worker has a new role.
