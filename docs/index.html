National Health Assurance & Public Financing Simulator

Bottom-up policy simulations you can interrogate: a fiscal model of the proposed National Health Assurance Framework — a public system replacing U.S. private health insurance with automatic coverage and zero point-of-care cost — and a general U.S. tax model showing how any funding need can be financed, and who bears the burden across income groups. Every parameter is sourced; every major assumption is adjustable. The models compute their own answers.

All results in real 2024 dollars. Calibrated to CMS National Health Expenditure data (2023, the last finalized year) and CBO's household income and federal tax distribution. Sources & methodology on GitHub.

What you'd pay for care

The framework's core design rule: covered medically necessary care costs $0 at the point of use — no deductibles, no copays, no surprise bills, no medical debt (patient billing is capped at 0.5% of covered encounters). The system is paid for through public financing instead, shown further down. Methodology, honestly

What this is: a national-aggregate annual model (v1). It computes the status-quo world and the NHA world category by category from CMS 2023 calibration data, phases policy in over the framework's Phase 0–8 roadmap, and samples every uncertain parameter from a sourced low/central/high range (triangular distribution, independent draws). Steady state is the 2040–2042 average.
On the framework document's original figure: earlier drafts asserted a mature cost of $4.75T/yr (2024 dollars) without a derivation. That figure is superseded by this model's computed projections — under central assumptions the model lands somewhat above it, and reaches it only under the optimistic scenario. The full comparison lives in the parameter table and scenario presets, not in the headline.
What it is not: it has no regional, demographic, or specialty-level detail (planned v2); it does not model macroeconomic feedback (wage pass-through, labor-market effects); parameter draws are independent, which understates tail risk; and access/health-outcome effects are represented only where they carry a cost.
Weakest assumptions — these parameters have no direct real-world source and materially move the result. They are flagged low in the controls and listed with their derivation anchors in the full parameter table below.

Tax model methodology

Structure: income groups follow CBO's distribution format (quintiles, 80–90th, 90–99th, top 1%). Each instrument carries a sourced revenue estimate at 2024 scale (growing with the economy), a phase-in schedule, and an incidence vector — who ultimately bears it — using CBO/JCT conventions (employer payroll → workers' wages; corporate → 25% labor / 75% capital; consumption taxes → consumption shares).
Sources: the income-group table is CBO's Distribution of Household Income, 2022 (published January 2026), extracted from CBO's own supplemental workbook and inflated to 2024 dollars; revenue scores come from CBO's Options for Reducing the Deficit: 2025–2034 (December 2024, JCT staff), the Treasury FY2025 Greenbook, and the Saez–Zucman wealth-tax letter; health spending by quintile is BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2024 (healthcare runs 20.7% of income in the lowest quintile vs 3.7% in the highest — the regressivity this system unwinds). Full citations in research file 06.
Honest limits: incidence is static (no behavioral responses beyond the haircuts already in each estimate); group boundaries don't capture within-group variation (a "middle quintile" family with high health costs gains far more than one without); the wealth tax has no official JCT/CBO score and its legal survival is genuinely uncertain (the framework carries a fallback matrix for that reason); the estate instrument has no current score against the post-2025 $15M exemption; and CBO's December 2024 option scores predate the July 2025 reconciliation act, so some would re-base lower today.
