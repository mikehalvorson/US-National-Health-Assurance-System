The system today

American healthcare is not one system. It is four payment systems stapled together, with your coverage determined mostly by your job, your age, and your income. About 157 million people get insurance through an employer, who pays most of the premium out of what would otherwise be wages. Medicare covers ~67 million people over 65 or disabled, funded by payroll taxes, general federal revenue, and enrollee premiums. Medicaid & CHIP cover ~72 million lower-income people, funded jointly by Washington and the states. Everyone also pays directly at the point of care: deductibles, copays, and uncovered bills totaled $506B in 2023. And ~26 million people have no coverage at all. Here is where the $4.87T came from and where it went. Hover any ribbon for the details.

What's wrong, by the numbers

The problem is not that America spends heavily on health; every rich country does. It's that America pays far more and gets less coverage, less security, and far more overhead. Every figure below is sourced (citations in the research repository).

The fixable part

Most of that dysfunction is structural rather than medical, which means it can be fixed by redesigning how money moves rather than by rationing care. Four levers do most of the work:

  • Administrative consolidation. One public payer and one claims rail replace ~900 insurers' duplicated overhead and the billing bureaucracy providers maintain to fight them. Recoverable savings estimates in the literature run $200 to $500B a year.
  • Drug-price negotiation and public manufacturing. The IRA already proved government negotiation works (CBO-scored, 38–79% price cuts on the first ten drugs). Applied system-wide against prices 2.78× the international average, plus a public manufacturer for essential generics, modeled on Civica Rx's real $30-a-vial insulin. The model tests net price cuts of 25 to 55%.
  • Hospital payment reform. Global budgets replace per-claim billing, ending facility-fee markups, related-party extraction, and a slice of the $76–101B/yr in scored low-value care, while readiness funding protects ERs and rural service lines instead of letting them close.
  • Front-door capacity. A community diagnostic-treatment network and specialist e-consults absorb the routine care that currently lands in $2,453-average ER visits and months-long specialist queues.

The bet behind this design: these levers can pay for most of covering everyone with no bills. Whether that holds is what the model on this page computes; it is not assumed.

The proposal: National Health Assurance

A single public system replacing employment-linked insurance entirely, the design this dashboard simulates:

  • Automatic, continuous coverage for every resident: no enrollment cliffs, no churn, no linkage to your job.
  • $0 at the point of care for covered medically necessary care; medical debt for covered care is prohibited (patient billing capped at 0.5% of encounters).
  • National drug purchasing through a real-time public pharmacy claims utility (replacing PBMs), plus a Public Medicines Corporation manufacturing essential generics.
  • Hospitals on global budgets under public-service charters, funded for readiness and essential service lines, not per-claim revenue.
  • A four-type community diagnostic-treatment network (the plan specifies at least 15,000 units, from pharmacy micro-clinics to rural enhanced and urban public-health units; the Physical Care chapter derives the need-based count) delivering same-day routine care.
  • A structured specialist backplane: complete workups and 24-hour e-consults before in-person referrals, with urgency classes and protected urgent capacity.
  • Expanded benefits phased in: long-term care (home-first), behavioral health, dental, vision, hearing, and EMS.
  • A 12-year gated rollout (2027–2038) with go/no-go phase gates, transition protections for patients, providers, and displaced workers, and independent oversight bodies.

Federalism by design

Federal, state, and tribal authority must be separated precisely

The federal statute should operate directly where national uniformity is indispensable, preserve traditional state health and safety functions, and recognize tribal sovereignty and federal trust duties. It cannot force state officers to administer a federal program.

Federal direct authority

One national floor and a direct operating fallback

  • Coverage, benefits, payment rights, financing, national data and claims standards
  • Federal institutions, appropriations, remedies, audit, and continuity duties
  • Narrow express preemption when a state rule materially blocks the federal guarantee
State authority preserved

Health, safety, licensure, and more-protective rules

  • Professional licensure, discipline, facility safety, public health, and corporate law

Tribal sovereignty and trust

Direct nation-to-nation implementation

From bill text to live authority

How implementation would be performed

The legal transition follows the same safety logic as the clinical rollout: create the successor authority, prove it can operate, migrate by certified wave, and retire the predecessor only after continuity evidence passes.

01Close the legal crosswalk

Legislative counsel maps every affected federal section and each state, territorial, and tribal interface to a named disposition.

02Enact the 19-title act

Rights, institutions, mandatory funding, revenue, remedies, formulas, defaults, and transition authority take effect on sequenced dates.

03Amend legacy code

Congress inserts conforming text, savings clauses, severability, transition rules, and revival fallbacks before any repeal can operate.

04Fund and appoint

Trust funds, direct spending, appropriations, offices, inspectors, scorekeepers, courts, succession, and staffing floors become operational.

05Rule and compact

Agencies use APA notice and comment; governments execute clear, funded, voluntary compacts with data, audit, cure, and exit terms.

06Run systems in parallel

Claims, records, pharmacy, hospitals, and eligibility migrate in certified waves while predecessor benefits and pending cases remain protected.

07Sunset only after Gate 8

Legacy authority closes only after record, financial, workforce, appeal, continuity, and fallback evidence is independently certified.

Constitutional architecture

What the Constitution permits, constrains, and could optionally guarantee

Article I

Enumerated federal powers

Anchor financing in the taxing and spending powers; regulate interstate healthcare markets; use necessary-and-proper means tied to those powers.

Tenth Amendment

No commandeering or coercion

Administer federal duties federally. State participation must use clear, prospective, noncoercive funding terms and a workable direct federal option.

Article II

Executive power remains presidential

After Trump v. Slaughter, officers within the President's general administrative control must be removable at will.

Fifth Amendment

Process, property, and equal treatment

Give notice, hearings, review, compensation where property is taken, and rational, nondiscriminatory transition rules for patients and institutions.

First Amendment and RFRA

Belief does not erase patient access

Draft conscience rules narrowly, protect individual rights, and ensure an effective alternative provider and emergency-care pathway.

Supremacy Clause

Express, bounded preemption

State law yields only where Congress validly says so or a true conflict exists; explicit savings clauses prevent accidental field preemption.

Methodology and limits

What this is: a national-aggregate annual model. It computes the status-quo world and the NHA world category by category from CMS 2023 calibration data, phases policy in over the Phase 0–8 roadmap, and samples every uncertain parameter from a sourced low/central/high range (triangular distribution). Draws share a systemic optimism/pessimism factor (weight 0.35): runs where benefit expansions overrun also tend to be runs where savings levers underdeliver, which widens the tails compared with independent sampling. Employer premium savings pass through to wages (adjustable, 40–95%, CBO convention) and the resulting income/payroll tax revenue feeds back into financing. Steady state is the 2040–2042 average.
On the original headline figure: earlier drafts asserted a mature cost of $4.75T/yr (2024 dollars) without a derivation. That figure is superseded by this model's computed projections; under central assumptions the model lands somewhat above it, and reaches it only under the optimistic scenario. The full comparison lives in the parameter table and scenario presets, not in the headline.
What it still is not: specialty-level queues and wait-time dynamics are not modeled, nor is provider behavioral response beyond the payment factor. Macroeconomic feedback stops at first-order wage pass-through with a fixed 28% marginal-rate revenue feedback; there are no general-equilibrium or labor-supply effects. The correlation structure is a single systemic factor, not a full covariance matrix. Geographic detail exists for the care network (see the Physical Care tab) but the fiscal model's costs and prices remain national averages. Health outcomes are displayed with sources in the section above, not converted into dollars. Demographic aging is decomposed and shown under the cost-path chart, but the model does not simulate cohorts individually.

Chapter 2 · Paying for it

The same care, financed differently

The healthcare chapter showed the system costs about what the country already spends. This chapter answers the harder question: who writes the checks. It starts with where the money actually is, builds tax packages that meet the funding goal without falling on lower incomes, and shows, bracket by bracket, how new taxes compare with the premiums and bills that disappear. The next chapter turns to the law that has to authorize all of it.

Start with where the money is

Any honest financing conversation starts here. American wealth has concentrated to a degree the words "top 1%" no longer describe; the action is inside the top tenth and hundredth of a percent. That is why the model on this page breaks the top 1% into five bands, grows their capital faster than the economy (because it does grow faster), and includes instruments aimed specifically at them.

Why the ultra-wealthy are hard to tax

  • Income is optional at the top. The tax code mostly taxes realized income, and a billionaire can simply not realize any: hold appreciating assets, borrow against them for spending, and let the step-up at death erase the gain entirely. "Buy, borrow, die" is why measured top-0.01% tax rates fall below the merely rich.
  • Valuation and liquidity. Private businesses, art, and carried interests are hard to appraise annually, and taxing them can force sales; every wealth-tax design fights this.
  • A planning industry. Grantor trusts, dynasty trusts, valuation discounts, and pass-through games exist to move wealth around any single-instrument tax; the estate tax raises a fifth of what a static calculation says it should.
  • Legal uncertainty. A federal wealth tax faces the Constitution's direct-tax apportionment clause, and taxing unrealized gains tests the 16th Amendment's meaning of income (Moore v. United States left the question open, not answered).
  • Enforcement asymmetry. Audit rates on the largest returns collapsed for a decade; the IRS was outgunned by the taxpayers with the most complex returns and the most to gain from opacity.

How it can realistically be done

  • Tax what cannot flee or hide. Withholding and third-party reporting on capital income, an uncapped payroll base, and transaction taxes collect at the source, before planning starts.
  • Close the death loophole first. Ending stepped-up basis and taxing gains at death removes the exit that makes deferral a permanent escape; every rate increase works better once it is shut.
  • Minimum taxes on comprehensive income. The billionaire minimum tax sidesteps game-by-game whack-a-mole by asking one question: did you pay at least 25% on your true economic gain?
  • Tax inheritances as income to heirs. Constitutionally clean (it is income), progressive by construction, and aimed at dynasties rather than founders.
  • Fund the referees. High-end enforcement collects tax already owed; the return on IRS funding at the top is documented and large.
  • Coordinate internationally and at the exit. Pillar-2 minimum taxes, information exchange, and a real exit tax shrink the run-away option.
  • Build a legal ladder, not a single rung. The plan's own contingency matrix: if the wealth tax falls in court, fall back to mark-to-market; then to realization-at-death plus minimum taxes. Every scenario below survives losing its most contested instrument.

It has been done before

America taxed its top earners far harder than it does now, for decades, through its strongest growth years: the top marginal rate stayed at 91 to 92 percent through the entire Eisenhower administration and did not fall below 70 percent until 1982. It is 37 percent today.

Chapter 4 · Physical care

Hospitals become public-service infrastructure, then care moves closer to home

The change is larger than building community clinics. It starts by removing hospitals from the logic of payer contracts, shareholder extraction, and profitable service selection. Hospitals receive mission-locked public-service charters and certified budgets; regional nonprofit administrations plan capacity and protect essential services. The four-unit diagnostic-treatment network then becomes the local front door, linked to hospitals for emergency, inpatient, surgical, and complex specialty care.

The starting problem

Hospital administration is optimized around revenue, not the human need for care

A hospital is not an ordinary market. A patient with a stroke, sepsis, trauma, or a dangerous pregnancy cannot postpone the purchase, compare a complete price, or judge staffing quality in advance. When the institution has an owner entitled to residual profit, the easiest ways to improve returns can conflict with the public mission: raise prices, favor lucrative procedures, reduce labor, load the property with debt or rent, and close services whose social value is greater than their margin.

Market failure

Patients cannot bargain in an emergency

Demand is involuntary, quality is hard to observe, and local consolidation can leave no meaningful alternative. A hospital can possess market power precisely when a person is least able to exercise consumer power.

Financialization

Private equity intensifies the extraction path

Acquisition debt, management fees, related-party transactions, sale-leasebacks, short holding periods, and exit targets can turn clinical capacity into a financial asset. The risk is borne by patients, workers, and the community if the facility weakens or closes.

Administrative load

Competing payers multiply nonclinical work

Contracting, coding, prior authorization, utilization review, collections, denials, and revenue-cycle teams consume labor that does not diagnose, treat, clean a room, or keep a service line ready.

Why profit maximization is the wrong governing rule for hospitals

Illness makes people dependent. A humane system therefore treats survival capacity, safe staffing, maternity care, psychiatric beds, trauma response, and rural access as obligations to people, not optional product lines. Surplus is useful when it becomes reserves, safer staffing, equipment, and replacement capital. It becomes harmful when an outside residual claimant can extract it while the community retains the clinical risk.

Important qualification: ownership is not a patient-level quality score. Not every for-profit hospital is unsafe, and nonprofit status alone does not prevent monopoly pricing, overcompensation, or weak community benefit. The remedy is a binding charter, transparent accounts, related-party limits, a locked public mission, and enforceable quality and continuity duties for every participating hospital.

Evidence: HHS ownership report; JAMA private-equity safety study; FTC/DOJ/HHS ownership inquiry.

Chapter 5 · Medicines

How can one public purchaser lower prices and keep essential drugs available?

National purchasing covers the complete formulary. The Public Medicines Corporation, or PMC, does something narrower: it creates permanent public capacity for 200 essential, shortage-prone, high-volume, and preparedness product families. This chapter separates those two levers and shows where each one applies.

A public option on the supply side

The PMC is bargaining power, backup capacity, and a manufacturer of last resort

A national buyer can negotiate only when it has legal authority, credible alternatives, and the ability to walk away without interrupting care. The PMC supplies that alternative for a controlled portfolio. It does not replace the complete private drug industry or relax FDA quality requirements.

Why public capacity is needed

The market can make a medicine cheap and still make its supply fragile

Generic competition lowers prices when several qualified suppliers remain. It can fail when low margins drive producers away, one quality problem idles a scarce sterile line, or a buyer cannot see inventory across the supply chain. FDA identifies manufacturing quality problems as the most common reason for shortages and cannot require a company to continue or expand production.

Continuity

Keep indispensable products in production

Standing public lines do not abandon older injectables, antidotes, or small markets solely because another product is more profitable.

Competition

Create a credible cost-plus alternative

A qualified public product gives the national purchaser a real fallback when a supplier rejects reasonable price or access terms.

Visibility

Connect demand, inventory, lots, and capacity

The pharmacy utility and national inventory view reveal shortages early enough to shift orders, release stock, or add production.

Preparedness

Maintain surge capability before an emergency

Antidotes, fluids, antibiotics, and other response products need validated processes and qualified inputs before demand spikes.

What the government does not produce

Chapter 8 · Authority

Who operates, decides, audits, and enforces

Care, money, law, facilities, information, and people all need institutional owners, kept deliberately separate so no single body both runs the system and grades it. This chapter lays out the full governance architecture: 82 entities under 9 governing bodies, each with a role, a reason, what it replaces, and who leads it. The next chapter shows how that distributed authority is engineered to survive a hostile administration.

The governance architecture

Replacing an industry means replacing its functions, and every function needs an owner. The plan defines 82 entities organized under 9 governing bodies. The design follows two principles throughout: separate operations from oversight (the department that pays claims never judges its own appeals), and make the system hard to sabotage but easy to repair (mandatory payment rails, fixed-term appointments, vacancy-proof duties, an independent scorekeeper with a public formula registry). Each diagram below is one governing body; click any box for the full entry, including the position in charge and what it answers for.

Leadership titles follow standard federal practice: Senate-confirmed heads for benefit-paying executive bodies, with nonexecutive scorekeeping and adjudication assigned only to constitutionally appropriate legislative offices or courts. After the Supreme Court's June 29, 2026 decision in Trump v. Slaughter, this dashboard does not assume that an executive regulator can be insulated from presidential removal. "Replaces" identifies the function absorbed, not a claim that the named organization vanishes on day one; the Transition body manages every handoff.

The threat: dismantlement without repeal

A hostile executive would not need to win an immediate vote to repeal National Health Assurance. It could try to make the entitlement fail in practice: hold back money, leave leadership posts empty, hollow out staffing, delay or withdraw rules, suppress performance evidence, decline enforcement, or let contractors and state partners obstruct essential systems. The first harms would appear as interrupted medication, unpaid providers, closed services, broken appeals, and missing public reports.

Design boundary

Make unilateral executive hollowing visible, temporary, and remediable. The architecture cannot prevent Congress from changing the statute or a court from invalidating an authority. It can prevent ordinary executive inaction from quietly operating as repeal and can preserve lawful continuity while Congress and courts respond.

No single official, office, rule, contract, or court route is expected to carry the whole defense. Select a layer to see the attack it blocks, the continuity action that occurs without permission from the obstructing official, and the independent body that checks the result.

From interference to continuity

The plan does not wait for a discretionary self-report from the same chain of command accused of obstruction. It specifies a detection-to-remedy workflow driven by clocks, logs, obligations, staffing records, public-data completeness, escrow status, and complaints.

Limits and unfinished statutory work

Open legal design

Leadership rules still require legislation

Governing-body composition, appointment, terms, removal, quorum, conflicts, vacancy, and succession are left for final statutory design. Trump v. Slaughter now forecloses ordinary for-cause insulation for officers within the President's general administrative control; every body still needs an Appointments Clause and separation-of-powers opinion.

Operational proof

A fallback on paper is not enough

Each fallback still needs authority, an owner, funds, staff, systems, data, contracts, an activation clock, exercises, and a return-and-reconciliation path. Legal contingency instruments and recovery-time objectives remain subjects for specialized analysis.

Constitutional boundary

Hardening is not permanent entrenchment

A later Congress may amend or repeal the statute, and courts may constrain particular authorities. The design instead makes those changes formal and reviewable while severability and scoped fallbacks preserve unrelated coverage, payment, rights, and continuity functions.

The United States has many capable electronic record systems, but not a dependable system-level record. Data exchange varies by vendor, payer, facility, and setting. A person can therefore have several incomplete versions of the same history while clinicians still lack the medication, result, discharge, or referral information needed for the next decision.

What the system stores

The plan defines logical records rather than a vendor-specific database. Every record carries its authority, steward, schema version, event and effective times, provenance, access class, consent or purpose, correction chain, quality flags, and retention state. That makes a clinical fact, payment, appeal, or published metric reproducible instead of silently overwritable.

Targets across the twelve-year rollout

Every KPP and TPP now carries a target at every phase where it is measurable, built from three sources in priority order: the plan's own gate floors and milestones (authoritative, shown below), the Data tab's hand-derived information-mesh plan (reused verbatim so the two tabs always agree), and published ramp rules for everything else. How derived interim targets are built. Maximize-percentage targets enter at a deficit of four times the maturity headroom (capped at 10 points) and tighten linearly; minimize targets (error rates, waits, cost ceilings) enter at a multiple of the mature ceiling and tighten geometrically; vs-baseline targets ramp as even fractions of the mature delta; and the seven "to be calibrated" outcome metrics carry qualitative obligations (baseline, trend, calibration, certification) because inventing numbers the plan deliberately deferred would be false precision. Each derived entry states its rule and why the metric becomes measurable at that phase.

People are the capacity system

This cannot be staffed by moving boxes on an organization chart

Coverage, facilities, medicines, records, and specialist routing work only when qualified people are available in the right role, region, modality, and schedule. The plan therefore treats workforce as a hard constraint: headcount is discounted for leave, competency, schedule availability, retention, burnout, and unsafe overtime before it counts as effective capacity.

01

National shortages are geographically unequal

Nonmetropolitan nursing gaps, specialist scarcity, and long travel distances can leave a nominally covered rural resident without usable care.

02

Rural readiness is larger than visit volume

A low-volume community still needs observation, emergency handoff, pharmacy, transport, maternal and pediatric triage, and weather resilience.

03

Training arrives years after funding

A funded slot becomes capacity only after faculty, clinical rotations, completion, credentialing, and a matched service location.

04

Burnout destroys nominal capacity

Vacancies, turnover, leave, moral injury, and excessive overtime reduce safe capacity even when an authorized position exists on paper.

05

New benefits release hidden demand

Long-term care, behavioral health, dental, vision, hearing, EMS, and public-health access cannot expand faster than trained teams and safe facilities.

06

Administrative simplification displaces workers

Private claims, prior authorization, PBM rebate, brokerage, and revenue-cycle work shrinks before every worker has a new role.

A rural staffing plan built around a permanent core

Travel staff are a relief valve, not the rural model. Every region first funds a permanent local core and the facilities, housing, supervision, transport, and family support that make retention possible. A public regional flex pool then covers vacancies, leave, training, seasonal demand, and short surges without forcing unsafe overtime.

01

Measure safe FTE need

Count readiness, leave, supervision, geography, weather, EMS handoff, and low-volume essential service.

02

03

Fund the permanent core

Use transparent rural premiums, call pay, housing, childcare, spouse support, schedule control, and local leadership.

04

Build the local pipeline

Reserve training slots, rural rotations, scholarships, community recruitment, and bounded service obligations with hardship rights.

05

Deploy the flex pool

RS-CORPS supplies rotating nurses, paramedics, imaging/lab staff, respiratory therapy, pharmacy, and behavioral health.

06

Bring specialists to the team

Use e-consults, tele-specialty, tele-radiology, tele-ICU, and cross-region queue capacity without double-counting supply.

07

Gate expansion on retention

Hold or resize benefit waves when vacancy, burnout, EMS, home-care, behavioral-health, or hospital staffing floors fail.

AI and telehealth add capacity; they do not erase bedside work

Technology removes avoidable clerical load, carries expertise across distance, and helps a small local team close the loop. It does not count as capacity twice, authorize unsafe staffing, or make final clinical decisions without an accountable professional.

AI-assisted workflow

Give time back to clinicians

  • Draft encounter documentation and patient instructions for human review.
  • Check referral packets, result closure, medication lists, and follow-up queues.
  • Forecast staffing, transport, inventory, and surge demand by region.
  • Support triage and translation while preserving override, audit, and manual fallback.
