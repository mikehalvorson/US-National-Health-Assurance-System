National Health Assurance Simulator

A bottom-up fiscal simulation of the proposed National Health Assurance Framework — a public system that would replace U.S. private health insurance with automatic coverage, zero point-of-care cost, national drug purchasing, hospital global budgets, and a community diagnostic-treatment network. Every parameter is sourced; every major assumption is adjustable. The model computes its own answer — it is not tuned to match the framework's claimed cost.

All results in real 2024 dollars. Calibrated to CMS National Health Expenditure data (2023, the last finalized year). Monte Carlo uncertainty bands from sourced parameter ranges. Sources & methodology on GitHub.

What you'd pay for care

The framework's core design rule: covered medically necessary care costs $0 at the point of use — no deductibles, no copays, no surprise bills, no medical debt (patient billing is capped at 0.5% of covered encounters). The system is paid for through public financing instead, shown further down. Methodology, honestly

What this is: a national-aggregate annual model (v1). It computes the status-quo world and the NHA world category by category from CMS 2023 calibration data, phases policy in over the framework's Phase 0–8 roadmap, and samples every uncertain parameter from a sourced low/central/high range (triangular distribution, independent draws). Steady state is the 2040–2042 average.
On the framework document's original figure: earlier drafts asserted a mature cost of $4.75T/yr (2024 dollars) without a derivation. That figure is superseded by this model's computed projections — under central assumptions the model lands somewhat above it, and reaches it only under the optimistic scenario. The full comparison lives in the parameter table and scenario presets, not in the headline.
What it is not: it has no regional, demographic, or specialty-level detail (planned v2); it does not model macroeconomic feedback (wage pass-through, labor-market effects); parameter draws are independent, which understates tail risk; and access/health-outcome effects are represented only where they carry a cost.
Weakest assumptions — these parameters have no direct real-world source and materially move the result. They are flagged low in the controls and listed with their derivation anchors in the full parameter table below.
